\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Korea's 2020 Public-Interest Direct Payment Scheme transfers 1.2~million~KRW per year to smallholders below a 0.5-ha cultivated-area cutoff under a per-farm flat-rate design, distinct from the area-proportional U.S.\ and EU CAP schedules \citep{Kirwan2009_rentcap, BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap}. We test the Agricultural Household Model's separability null at this cutoff via a difference-in-differences regression-discontinuity design on the Farm Household Economic Survey panel ($N = 11{,}010$ farm-years, $2{,}776$ farms, 2018--2022), applying symmetric observable-eligibility screening on both sides. Two AHM extensions yield falsifiable predictions: wealth-biased liquidity \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity} predicts a monotone-in-tenancy gradient on own-cultivated area (F1, primary); implicit-labor supervision \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision} predicts a hired-labor margin response (F2, auxiliary).

The pure-tenant own-area response is $+1{,}151$~m\textsuperscript{2} at the T2 bandwidth ($p = .036$), monotone-decreasing across four non-pure-owner bins ($+1{,}151 \to +438 \to +258 \to -52 \to 0$). The per-farm flat-rate design reverses the rent-cost pass-through documented for area-proportional schedules: pass-through is $-13.7\%$ at T1 ($p < .10$), against $+25\%$ in the U.S.\ \citep{Kirwan2009_rentcap} and $+46$--$55\%$ in the EU \citep{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap}. The operating-cost-net-of-rent response is $-3.57$~M~KRW at T1, supportive of the $(S,s)$ inaction reading (not individually significant after multiple-testing correction). We reject AHM separability at the 0.5~ha cutoff for the credit-constrained non-pure-owner subpopulation via the wealth-biased liquidity channel; the per-farm flat-rate payment design itself is the policy lever that severs the per-acre landlord-capture channel.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Agricultural Household Model; separability; regression discontinuity; difference-in-differences; direct payment; smallholder; liquidity; Korea.

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================
\label{sec:intro}

Does the Agricultural Household Model's separability null hold for Korean smallholders at the 0.5~ha cutoff of a per-farm flat-rate direct payment, and through which mechanism does it fail if rejected? Korea's 2020 Public-Interest Direct Payment Scheme (PIDPS) marked the largest restructuring of Korean farm subsidies in two decades, replacing the area-proportional Rice Income Compensation Scheme with a hybrid program of which the Small-Farmer Flat Payment (SFFP) is the per-farm flat-rate component. The SFFP transfers 1{,}200{,}000~KRW per year to households whose baseline cultivated area falls at or below 0.5~ha ($5{,}000$ m\textsuperscript{2});\footnote{The 2020 launch amount was 1.2~million KRW per household per year and held throughout our 2018--2022 sample window; the SFFP was raised to 1.3~million KRW effective 2025. All magnitudes in this paper anchor to the 1.2-million-KRW period.} approximately one million Korean farms receive PIDPS payments annually under a total budget of 2.3 trillion KRW, of which the SFFP accounts for roughly twenty percent. Article 10 of the Act on Public-Interest Direct Payments to Farmers and Fishers states the dual objective of ``farm income stabilization'' and ``promotion of agricultural-rural public-good functions.'' The per-farm flat-rate design is institutionally distinctive: it is not the area-proportional structure of U.S.\ crop subsidies \citep{Kirwan2009_rentcap} or the EU Common Agricultural Policy Pillar I \citep{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap}, with the closest cousin a Swiss spatial-RD on a similarly national, non-supranational direct-payment scheme \citep{ZimmertZorn2022_swissrd}.

The Agricultural Household Model (AHM) separability null states that, under complete markets, smallholder production decisions---land tenure, capital investment, labor allocation---are recursively factorizable from consumption decisions, so a lump-sum transfer enters only the consumption sub-problem and leaves production unaffected \citep{SinghSquireStrauss1986_ahm, deJanvryFafchampsSadoulet1991_peasant}. We test this null at the 0.5~ha SFFP cutoff. The separability-test literature \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} has typically rejected separability using demographic shocks as instruments in developing-country settings; we instead exploit a sharp developed-country policy cutoff. Our framework formalizes two AHM extensions through which separability fails. The \textbf{wealth-biased liquidity} channel \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity} is primary (Channel A): credit access is increasing in baseline wealth, and the SFFP transfer relaxes the rental-versus-ownership margin asymmetrically across the tenancy distribution. The \textbf{implicit-labor supervision} channel \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision} is auxiliary (Channel B): the household's shadow wage diverges from the market wage when own-family labor is imperfectly substitutable with hired labor. The bargaining and rental-incidence margin of price adjustment enters the analysis as a reduced-form aggregate-equilibrium implication outside the household-model core; we discuss it in \S\ref{sec:discussion} rather than as a primary theoretical prediction. We pre-specify two falsification triggers under this scope: F1, a monotone tenancy gradient in own-cultivated area (load-bearing rejection trigger for the wealth-bias channel); F2, a hired-labor margin response on off-farm income (informative on the supervision channel, not rejection-bearing under sign indeterminacy in the EK derivation).\footnote{The theoretical scope, outcome hierarchy, and F1/F2 pre-specifications were fixed prior to estimation; the corresponding pre-specification archive is bundled in the replication package.}

Our contribution stands against four reference points. The U.S.\ and EU literatures on area-proportional direct-payment incidence---\citet{Kirwan2009_rentcap} documenting $\approx 25\%$ rent-capitalization of the U.S.\ Direct and Counter-Cyclical Program and \citet{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap} reporting $9.1$--$55\%$ pass-through of EU CAP Pillar~I---establish a developed-country prior of substantial landlord rent extraction under per-acre design. The SFFP's per-farm flat-rate design severs that channel by construction; our framework treats rental incidence as an aggregate-equilibrium implication outside the household-model core. The \citet{Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment} decoupling-induced disinvestment finding is the closest theoretical lineage for our operating-cost sub-prediction (when transfers are small relative to lumpy adjustment costs, the $(S,s)$ inaction band deepens and operating expenses fall; \citealp{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy}); we extend the lineage by formalizing the wealth-bias signature (the Carter--Olinto cross-partial) the disinvestment literature does not isolate. Closest in theoretical structure to ours is \citet{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}, whose rural-Paraguay land-titling evidence we replicate in a developed-country direct-payment context with sharper identification. If both F1 and F2 fail to fire, the joint null becomes a precise complement to \citet{LaFaveThomas2016_markets}---smallholder separability surviving where labor and credit markets approach completeness.

We identify the treatment effect via a difference-in-differences regression-discontinuity (DiD-RD) design exploiting the 0.5~ha SFFP cutoff, with the running variable $rv_{2018,i} = A_{2018,i} - 5{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2} frozen at the 2018 baseline to rule out post-policy manipulation (\S\ref{sec:institutional-eligibility}). Treatment $D_i$ is the conjunction of the three FHES-observable SFFP criteria; we apply this screening symmetrically on both sides of the cutoff, yielding a sample of $N = 11{,}010$ farm-years across $2{,}776$ farms in which $D_i = 1 \iff rv_{2018,i} \le 0$ on the screened subpopulation. The estimand is an ITT, not a sharp ATT, since administrative compliance on the five unobservable criteria is $\approx 92\%$ (\S\ref{sec:institutional-identification}). The full identification specification---bandwidth grid (T1/T2/T3), cluster-robust + wild cluster bootstrap inference, and Holm step-down across the primary outcome family---appears in \S\ref{sec:identification}. The primary outcomes are own-cultivated area ($A_{\text{own}}$) and operating cost net of rent ($\text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}$); the auxiliary outcome is off-farm income (F2 supervision-margin diagnostic).

F1 predicts a monotone-in-baseline-tenancy gradient in $\partial A_{\text{own},i} / \partial T_{SFFP}$: pure-tenant smallholders with the lowest baseline ownership share exhibit the largest response; pure-owner smallholders anchored at $s_0 = 1$ exhibit zero response by construction (the five-bin partition over $s_0 = A_{\text{own},2018}/A_{2018}$ is pre-specified in \S\ref{sec:liquidity}). The transfer-to-adjustment-cost ratio $T_{SFFP}/\tau \in [0.024, 0.048]$ (\S\ref{sec:calibration}) places the cohort within the $(S,s)$ inaction band of \citet{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy}-type models, supporting the operating-cost sub-prediction $\beta_{\text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}} \le 0$. The \S\ref{sec:results} estimates confirm the F1 monotone tenancy gradient (pure-tenant own-area response $+1{,}151$~m\textsuperscript{2} at T2, $p = .036$; low-owner $+438$~m\textsuperscript{2}, $p = .047$) and a $-3.57$~M~KRW operating-cost-net-of-rent response at T1 supportive of the $(S,s)$ reading. The rent-pass-through reduced form is $-13.7\%$ at T1 ($p < .10$), reversing the U.S. ($+25\%$, \citealp{Kirwan2009_rentcap}) and EU CAP ($+46$--$55\%$, \citealp{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap}) benchmarks.

This paper makes three contributions. \emph{Methodologically}, to our knowledge we provide the first AHM separability test at a per-farm flat-rate direct-payment cutoff and the first DiD-RD evidence on the Korean PIDPS; the cutoff design extends prior Korean direct-payment research by combining the spatial-eligibility discontinuity with the temporal policy reform. We further restore methodological purity by aligning the empirical treatment indicator with the policy definition of SFFP eligibility: rather than relying on the area cutoff alone, we estimate the ITT on the conjunction of all three FHES-observable statutory criteria and document the $14.6\%$ area-eligible-but-statutorily-ineligible margin separately (\S\ref{sec:robustness-areaonly} reports the area-only-treatment robustness comparison). \citet{ChoiJodlowski2025_landreg} study land-ownership regulation in Korea but not the SFFP subsidy at a cutoff; \citet{ChoiMun2025_pidps_offinc} apply an off-farm-income RDD at the same 0.5~ha threshold but do not combine the DiD-RD design with an AHM-separability test. \emph{Theoretically}, we offer an AHM-extension framework with two non-separabilities (wealth-biased liquidity and implicit-labor supervision) that yields closed-form falsifiable predictions, alongside an explicit ex-theory disclosure on the rental-incidence margin (\S\ref{sec:theory}~equilibrium-rent caveat). \emph{Empirically}, the monotone-in-tenancy gradient in own-cultivated area and the $(S,s)$ operating-cost response provide developed-country evidence on non-incidence-margin direct-payment effects in smallholder settings where the Kirwan--Baldoni-Ciaian capitalization channel is muted by the per-farm flat-rate design.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. \S\ref{sec:institutional} details the institutional context. \S\ref{sec:theory} develops the conceptual framework. \S\ref{sec:data} describes the data. \S\ref{sec:identification} specifies the empirical strategy. \S\ref{sec:results} reports headline results. \S\ref{sec:robustness} conducts robustness checks. \S\ref{sec:discussion} discusses policy and theoretical implications and concludes.

\section{Institutional Context}
% =============================================================================
\label{sec:institutional}

\subsection{The 2020 PIDPS Reform and the Two-Branch Schedule}
\label{sec:institutional-reform}

The Public-Interest Direct Payment Scheme (PIDPS), effective May 1, 2020, consolidates the Rice Income Compensation Scheme and a collection of complementary direct-payment programs that had accumulated through the 2000s and 2010s into a single two-branch design under Article 10 of the Act on Public-Interest Direct Payments to Farmers and Fishers. The two branches are mutually exclusive at the household level: an SFFP-eligible household does NOT receive ABP, and vice versa. The Small-Farmer Flat Payment (SFFP) transfers 1{,}200{,}000~KRW per household per year, independent of cultivated area, crop choice, or output, to households satisfying the eight eligibility requirements detailed in \S\ref{sec:institutional-eligibility}. The Area-Based Payment (ABP), applicable to households not qualifying for the SFFP, pays a per-hectare amount that varies by farm-type tier: 2{,}050{,}000~KRW/ha for paddy-rice on protected-zone land, 1{,}970{,}000~KRW/ha for non-protected paddy and upland fields, and 1{,}890{,}000~KRW/ha for marginal-land tiers.\footnote{The geographic protection designations follow MAFRA Notice 2020-23 and are stable through our sample window.} At the 0.5-hectare cutoff, the marginal SFFP-eligible household receives the 1{,}200{,}000~KRW flat amount. The counterfactual ABP transfer for a household just above the cutoff (i.e., area $> 0.5$~ha) is approximately 985{,}000~KRW (computed at the 1{,}970{,}000~KRW/ha base rate on $0.5$~ha), yielding a discrete expected-transfer jump of approximately 215{,}000~KRW per year at the cutoff.

\subsection{SFFP Eligibility Requirements and Observable Compliance}
\label{sec:institutional-eligibility}

SFFP eligibility is conditional on eight requirements that the household must jointly satisfy: (i)~total cultivated area at or below 0.5~hectares; (ii)~household-aggregate owned farmland below 1.55~hectares; (iii)~the household head's farming tenure of three years or more; (iv)~rural-residence tenure of three years or more; (v)~individual off-farm income below 20~million~KRW per year (assessed per household member); (vi)~total household off-farm income below 45~million~KRW per year (aggregated across members); (vii)~registration in the National Agricultural Products Quality Management Service (NAQS) Farm Business Registry; and (viii)~compliance with seventeen public-interest provisions covering soil management, water quality, agricultural-chemical disclosure, and biodiversity. Non-compliance with~(viii) reduces disbursement by 10~percent rather than disqualifying the household. The household-definition rule applicable to criteria~(ii) and~(vi) pools spouses, unmarried children under nineteen, and children who left the household within the past three years---an anti-spinoff provision that closes the within-family separation loophole.

Of these eight requirements, the 0.5-hectare cutoff~(i) is the only sharp threshold tied to a continuous, pre-2020-fixed observable: 2018-baseline cultivated area in the Statistics Korea Farm Household Economic Survey (FHES). Requirement~(ii) (owned-area below 1.55~hectares) is observable in the FHES once the land registry is re-aggregated across the household-pooling rule, and requirement~(vi) (total household off-farm income) is observable directly from the FHES income module. Requirements~(iii)--(v), (vii), and~(viii) are administered through MAFRA local-office records, the NAQS registry, and aT disbursement audits, and are not present in survey data.

Within our treated cohort---the 1{,}325 households whose 2018 baseline cultivated area falls at or below 0.5~hectares---1{,}131 (85.4\%) also satisfy the FHES-observable requirements~(ii) and~(vi); the remaining 194 households (14.6\%) fail one of these two. The owned-area ceiling~(ii) binds for 1.8\% of the treated cohort, and the off-farm-income ceiling~(vi) binds for 12.8\%.\footnote{The 12.8\% off-farm-income margin is concentrated among households with one or more members in formal off-farm employment whose annual labor income alone approaches the 45-million-KRW ceiling.} The 14.6\% non-compliance figure is a conservative lower bound on actual non-receipt at the cutoff: it omits the attrition that would arise from the unobservable requirements~(iii)--(v), (vii), and~(viii). Administrative receipt at the cutoff is therefore meaningfully below one.

\subsection{Identification Posture and Per-Farm Flat-Rate Leverage}
\label{sec:institutional-identification}

We frame the DiD-RD estimator of \S\ref{sec:identification} as an intent-to-treat (ITT) effect on the symmetric-screened sample. The 0.5-hectare cutoff is the only policy-defined sharp threshold in the eligibility schedule, and 2018-baseline cultivated area is anti-manipulation by construction---households did not anticipate the May 2020 reform when allocating land in 2018. The main analysis applies the same (ii)+(vi) observable-eligibility screening on both sides of the cutoff (\S\ref{sec:identification}), producing a sample of 2{,}776 farms in which $D_i = 1 \iff rv_{2018,i} \le 0$. The asymmetric-sample variant (which screens only the treated side, dropping 194 treated-but-ineligible households) is reported as a robustness check in \S\ref{sec:robustness-asymmetric}; the area-only design (which applies no observable screening) is reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness-areaonly}. Both alternative samples preserve the F1 monotone-gradient sign on $A_{\text{own}}$ and the direction of the operating-cost-net-of-rent response. We do not estimate the sharp ATT, since the unobservable requirements (iii)--(v), (vii), (viii) are administratively enforced and not present in survey data; the ITT integrates over administrative non-receipt within the screened-eligible population.

The per-farm flat-rate SFFP design is the central institutional source of identification leverage relative to the U.S. and EU direct-payment literatures. \citet{Kirwan2009_rentcap} documents that approximately 25\% of area-proportional U.S. Direct and Counter-Cyclical Program payments are capitalized into farmland rental rates, an incidence result driven by the per-acre design of the U.S. payment schedule. \citet{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap} reports rental-rate pass-through from EU CAP Pillar~I payments of 9.1--46.2\% in the short run and 11--55\% in the long run, again driven by the per-hectare structure. The SFFP departs from these designs: a household receives the same 1.2-million-KRW flat amount whether it cultivates 100~m\textsuperscript{2} or 4{,}900~m\textsuperscript{2}, eliminating the per-acre capitalization channel by construction. Our \S\ref{sec:robustness} estimate of a $-12.0\%$ rent-cost pass-through at the T2 bandwidth is institutionally consistent with the absence of this channel; the remaining margins of household response---wealth-biased liquidity (\S\ref{sec:theory} Channel~A) and implicit-labor supervision (\S\ref{sec:theory} Channel~B)---are the focus of our test of AHM separability.

Data on program rules and budget allocations come from MAFRA administrative records, eligibility verification and farm-business registration come from the NAQS, and SFFP disbursement records are maintained by the Korea Agricultural Trade Corporation (aT). The Statistics Korea FHES, our analysis dataset, is independent of these administrative sources; we describe the FHES sample in \S\ref{sec:data}.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Baseline AHM and the Separability Null}
\label{sec:ahm-baseline}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We test the \citet{SinghSquireStrauss1986_ahm} Agricultural Household Model (AHM) separability null by exploiting Korea's 0.5 ha eligibility cutoff for the per-farm flat-rate Public-Interest Direct Payment Scheme (PIDPS) Small-Farmer Flat Payment (SFFP). Two AHM extensions---\textbf{wealth-biased liquidity} \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity} (extending the \citealp{Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment} decoupling-disinvestment lineage) and \textbf{implicit-labor supervision} \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}---yield independent margins through which separability fails.

\paragraph{Setup.} The household maximizes utility over consumption $C$ and leisure $L_\ell$,
\begin{equation}
\max_{C, L_f, L_o, A_{own}, A_{rent}, K} \; U(C, L_\ell),
\label{eq:ahm-objective}
\end{equation}
subject to the integrated farm-household budget constraint,
\begin{equation}
P_c\, C \;=\; P_q\, F(L_f, A, K) \;+\; w\, L_o \;-\; r\, A_{rent} \;-\; p_K\, I(K) \;+\; T_i,
\label{eq:ahm-budget}
\end{equation}
the time-allocation constraint $L_f + L_o + L_\ell = \bar L$, and the land identity $A_i = A_{own,i} + A_{rent,i}$. The household-specific transfer $T_i = T_{SFFP} \cdot D_i$ with policy scale $T_{SFFP} = 1{,}200{,}000$ KRW/year enters disposable income if and only if the household is SFFP-eligible (baseline area $A_{2018,i} \le 5{,}000$ m\textsuperscript{2}, equivalently treatment dummy $D_i = 1$); we use this notation uniformly across \S\ref{sec:theory} and Online Appendix \S B (cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:notation}).

\paragraph{Separability theorem.} Under complete markets---credit, insurance, labor, land all frictionless and competitively priced---the program in (\ref{eq:ahm-objective})--(\ref{eq:ahm-budget}) admits a recursive factorization \citep{SinghSquireStrauss1986_ahm}. The household first maximizes farm profit
\begin{equation}
\pi^* \;=\; \max_{L_f, A, K} \; \left\{ P_q\, F(L_f, A, K) - w\, L_f - r\, A_{rent} - p_K\, I(K) \right\}
\label{eq:profit-max}
\end{equation}
treating prices as parametric, then maximizes utility
\begin{equation}
\max_{C, L_o} \; U(C, L_\ell) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad P_c\, C \;=\; \pi^* + w(\bar L - L_\ell) + T_i
\label{eq:utility-max}
\end{equation}
taking $\pi^*$ as exogenous. A lump-sum transfer $T_i$ enters \emph{only} (\ref{eq:utility-max}); production decisions are unaffected. We therefore obtain the AHM separability null (varying the policy parameter $T_{SFFP}$)
\begin{equation}
H_0: \quad \begin{aligned}[t]
  \frac{\partial A_{own}}{\partial T_{SFFP}}
  \;&=\; \frac{\partial L_f}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \\
  \;&=\; \frac{\partial K}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \\
  \;&=\; \frac{\partial L_o}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;=\; 0.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:null}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Sources of separability failure.} \citet{deJanvryFafchampsSadoulet1991_peasant} catalogue four canonical sources of separability failure---missing credit, missing insurance, missing labor markets, missing land markets---each of which replaces a parametric market price in (\ref{eq:profit-max}) with an endogenous shadow price tied to household preferences. The separability-test literature \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} has established demographic shocks as instruments for rejecting (\ref{eq:null}); we instead exploit a sharp policy cutoff. Our two extensions in \S\ref{sec:liquidity}--\S\ref{sec:supervision} formalize the credit-imperfection and labor-imperfection margins respectively, with closed-form predictions whose Lagrangian derivations appear in the Online Appendix \S B.1--B.2.

\paragraph{Estimand and pre-period inference.} The DiD-RD design (\S\ref{sec:identification}) exploits the 2018-baseline frozen cutoff at $5{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2}; the recovered estimand is therefore an intent-to-treat (ITT) over \textit{eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area}. Throughout \S\ref{sec:theory}, the comparative-static objects $\partial A_{own,i}/\partial T_{SFFP}$, $\partial \text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}_i/\partial T_{SFFP}$, and $\partial \text{off\_farm\_income}_i/\partial T_{SFFP}$ should be read as the eligibility-induced effects on the post-period outcomes, robust to post-2020 threshold-crossings being endogenous. Parallel-trends inference rests on the 2018--2019 pre-period gap; following \citet{Roth2022_pretrends}, single-pre-period pretests do not certify the parallel-trends restriction and we therefore report \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid} sensitivity bounds (HonestDiD $\bar M$) on $\hat\beta_1$ and $\hat\beta_3$ in the robustness appendix. We also pre-empt the anticipation-effects threat: PIDPS was enacted in 2019 and took effect 2020-05-01, after the 2018 FHES baseline, so pre-2018 strategic area reporting could in principle have biased eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area if Korean smallholders had anticipated the 0.5~ha threshold prior to the Act's formal announcement. We argue against this concern on three grounds: (i) the per-farm flat-rate SFFP component of PIDPS was specifically designed after 2018 (no pre-announcement of a 0.5~ha cutoff during the FHES baseline window), (ii) we verify continuity of the cutoff density (no evidence of running-variable manipulation) via the \citet{McCrary2008_density} density-discontinuity test, with the estimate reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness}, and (iii) HonestDiD $\bar M$ bounds on $\hat\beta_1$ remain interpretable under bounded pre-period anticipation per \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid}.

\subsection{Wealth-Biased Liquidity Relaxation}
\label{sec:liquidity}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Imperfection.} Smallholders face a wealth-dependent credit access function $\rho(W_i)$ with $\rho'(W_i) > 0$: wealthier households obtain credit at lower marginal cost \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}. This imperfection is binding empirically in the developing- and lower-middle-income agricultural literature \citep{PittKhandker1998_credit, FosterRosenzweig1995_learning}, and---we argue---in the Korean smallholder context, where farms with small baseline asset bases (predominantly tenants) cannot freely borrow against future rental cash flows to finance land purchase.

\textbf{Asset-threshold rule.} For households at the rental--ownership margin, the indifference condition between renting and buying land yields a wealth threshold $W^*(\mathbf{p})$ as a function of prices $\mathbf{p}$ (rent $r$, land price $p_{land}$, interest rate $R$). A household owns land iff
\begin{equation}
W_i + \frac{T_i}{1-\delta} \;\ge\; W^*(\mathbf{p}),
\label{eq:CO-threshold}
\end{equation}
where $T_i = T_{SFFP} \cdot D_i$ is the household-specific subsidy flow (cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:notation}) and $\delta$ is the discount factor. The subsidy enters as a permanent-income-equivalent shift in disposable wealth, lowering the effective threshold $W^*$ by the amount $T_i/(1-\delta)$ for SFFP-eligible households.

\textbf{Closed-form predictions} (derivations in Online Appendix \S B.1; \citealp{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}; \citealp{Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment}):
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{\partial A_{own,i}}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;=\; -\,\varphi(W_i^*) \cdot \frac{\partial W^*}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;>\; 0,}
\label{eq:CO-1}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi(W^*) > 0$ is the density of household wealth at the threshold.

\paragraph{Aggregation note.} Equation (\ref{eq:CO-1}) is a population-aggregate threshold-crossing magnitude written at the individual indexed by $i$ for compactness; the implied estimand is $\partial \mathbb{E}[A_{own,i} \mid \text{eligibility window}]/\partial T_{SFFP}$, i.e., an average over the $\varphi(W_i^*)$-weighted marginal types. The DiD-RD specification in \S\ref{sec:identification} recovers this aggregate marginal effect as the local average treatment effect at the cutoff, not a household-specific partial derivative. Pure tenants ($s_{0,i} = A_{own,2018,i}/A_{2018,i} = 0$) face the largest sub-threshold mass within the eligibility window, while pure owner-operators ($s_{0,i} = 1$) sit inframarginally above $W^*$ at baseline and exhibit zero response by construction. Operationalized as a discrete five-bin partition with pure\_owner as the inframarginal reference (cf.\ Online Appendix \S B.1),
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\begin{aligned}
&\Delta A_{own,b} > \Delta A_{own,b'} > 0
   \;\text{ for }\; b \prec b' \;\text{ in }\; \{\text{pure tenant},\, \text{low-owner},\, \text{mixed},\, \text{high-owner}\}, \\
&\text{with}\; \Delta A_{own,\text{pure owner}} = 0,
\end{aligned}}
\label{eq:CO-2}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta A_{own,b}$ is the bin-$b$ post-policy change in own area per (\ref{eq:CO-1}). This five-bin discrete monotonicity in baseline tenancy (with pure\_owner $\equiv 0$ as the inframarginal reference and the four non-pure-owner bins ordered by $s_{0,i}$) is the empirical signature uniquely diagnostic of wealth-biased liquidity and cannot arise from a universal income-effect channel (under which $\Delta A_{own,b}$ would be uniform across bins).

\textbf{Capital-adjustment sub-prediction.} Conditional on $A_{own}$ pivot, the credit-constrained capital FOC implies an adjustment threshold $\tau$ on physical investment. If $T_{SFFP}/\tau < 1$---i.e., the subsidy is small relative to the discrete capital adjustment scale---no within-household investment crossing occurs and the marginal transfer is absorbed on non-capital margins:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{\partial \text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}_i}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;\le\; 0 \quad \text{if } T_{SFFP}/\tau < 1.}
\label{eq:CO-3}
\end{equation}
This adjustment-threshold logic parallels the $(S,s)$ inaction band of \citet{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy} cast within an AHM-internal credit constraint; we take the predictions of \citet{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity} and \citet{Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment} as load-bearing rather than the macro lumpy-investment framework, which is outside our $\alpha$-strict AHM-extension scope.

\subsection{Implicit Labor with Supervision}
\label{sec:supervision}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Imperfection.} Labor-market completeness fails when hired labor requires costly supervision \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}. Each hour of hired labor carries a monitoring cost $m > 0$ borne by the farm operator, driving a wedge between the shadow wage of family labor $w_f$ and the market wage $w_m$,
\begin{equation}
w_{f,i} \;=\; w_m + m \cdot \mathbf{1}[\ell_{h,i} > 0],
\label{eq:EK-wedge}
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{h,i}$ is hired labor demand. When family labor is the marginal type ($\ell_{h,i} > 0$), $w_f$ exceeds $w_m$ by the supervision wedge; allocations of family vs.\ hired vs.\ off-farm labor become non-separable from household preferences.

\textbf{Closed-form prediction} (derivation in Online Appendix \S B.2):
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{\partial \text{off\_farm\_income}_i}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;\ne\; 0 \quad \text{under non-separability,}}
\label{eq:EK-1}
\end{equation}
with sign determined by the comparative statics of the supervision-cost wedge as the budget constraint slackens. The sign is theoretically indeterminate ex ante because the transfer relaxes both the family-time scarcity (favoring more off-farm labor) and the cash constraint that drives supervised hiring (favoring less off-farm labor as own-farm activity expands); empirical estimation must resolve which dominates.

\textbf{Auxiliary status.} Channel~\ref{sec:supervision} is auxiliary to the wealth-biased liquidity channel of \S\ref{sec:liquidity}. Its rejection does not collapse the contribution: the primary $A_{own}$ gradient (\ref{eq:CO-1})--(\ref{eq:CO-2}) survives independently. Rather, a non-zero $\hat\beta_4$ on $\text{off\_farm\_income}$ would constitute a second independent rejection of separability via a labor-imperfection margin, strengthening the joint test.

\subsubsection*{Magnitude calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

The closed-form predictions in (\ref{eq:CO-1})--(\ref{eq:CO-3}) and (\ref{eq:EK-1}) hold under the regularity conditions C1--C5 stated in Online Appendix \S B.1, plus a numerical condition on $T_{SFFP}/\tau$ that locates the cohort within the $(S,s)$ inaction band. We anchor $\tau$ to Korean small-farm machinery capital cost via two independent sources. \citet{KREI_prodcost_2022} reports a purchase-price midpoint of approximately ${\sim}50$M~KRW for the dominant small-farm paddy-rice machinery class (30~HP-class tractor plus walking combine), and \citet{KAMICO_pricelist_2022} corroborates this anchor at the industry-price level for the 25--40~HP segment.\footnote{The two figures of ${\sim}50$M~KRW (purchase-price) and ${\sim}25$M~KRW (down-payment-equivalent) referenced across the literature on Korean farm-equipment financing reconcile under typical 50\% loan-to-value installment terms: a 50M~KRW machine financed at 50\% LTV requires a 25M~KRW down payment, with the balance amortized over the equipment lifetime. Both endpoints are used in the robustness range below.}

\paragraph{Transfer-to-adjustment-cost ratio.} At the SFFP scale of $T_{SFFP} = 1{,}200{,}000$~KRW/year, the ratio $T_{SFFP}/\tau$ falls in the range $[0.024, 0.048]$ across the down-payment-equivalent and purchase-price endpoints. Both endpoints sit comfortably inside the 5--10\% inaction band of \citet{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy}-type lumpy-investment models; the larger-$\tau$ endpoint deepens the (S,s) inaction implication and strengthens (\ref{eq:CO-3}). The qualitative prediction $\beta_3 \le 0$ holds across the robustness range $\tau \in [20\text{M}, 40\text{M}]$~KRW. The annual-flow interpretation of $T_{SFFP}$ (per-year transfer) and the lump-sum interpretation of $\tau$ (one-shot adjustment) make $T_{SFFP}/\tau$ measure per-period transfer scale relative to a one-shot capital threshold; under a multi-year horizon the cumulative transfer-to-adjustment ratio rises to $\approx 0.12$--$0.24$ over five years, still within the inaction band.

\paragraph{Sample-margin cross-references.} Among SFFP-eligible households ($D_i = 1$ in the post-period), 100\% appear with positive imputed payment in the analysis panel, reflecting eligibility-driven imputation (imputed SFFP $=$ 1.2~M~KRW $\cdot \mathbf{1}[D_i = 1, \text{Post}]$).\footnote{The administrative receipt rate among eligible households is approximately 92.3\% (FHES Wave 1 prior descriptives); the 7.7\% non-receipt margin reflects compliance lapses orthogonal to the eligibility cutoff. The DiD-RD design identifies an ITT under the imputation/eligibility universality; the receipt rate enters as a robustness margin only.} The hired-labor share is approximately 17--34\% across the eligibility window, with the upper tail concentrated among non-pure-owner subgroups (FHES Wave 1 raw farm-budget reporting; full descriptive in \S\ref{sec:data}). Measurement error in $s_{0,i}$---a 2018-baseline ratio constructed from FHES Wave~1 reported areas---is attenuated by the five-bin discreteness of the partition rule; bin-misclassification probabilities are small under FHES reporting accuracy and the F1 monotone-gradient test is robust to attenuation bias of this magnitude.

\paragraph{Pre-registered falsification.} We pre-specify two falsification predictions under this AHM-extension framework. We use \emph{F fires} to denote the trigger condition being met---i.e., the prediction is rejected by the data. The list is exhaustive on the F1--F2 outcomes; F1 alone is rejection-bearing for the AHM-separability test.

\begin{description}
\item[\textbf{F1 (load-bearing rejection trigger).}] Falsifies the wealth-biased liquidity channel \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}. F1 fires iff (i) the pure-tenant own-area coefficient $\hat\beta_{1,\text{tenant}}$ at T2 is statistically indistinguishable from zero ($p > .10$)---i.e., the credit-constrained subgroup does not increase own-cultivated area---\emph{and} (ii) the five-bin point estimates of $\hat\beta_1$ across $s_{0,i} \in \{0,\ (0,.33],\ (.33,.67],\ (.67,1),\ 1\}$ at T2 fail to be weakly monotone-decreasing in $s_{0,i}$ (with pure\_owner $\equiv 0$ as inframarginal reference; strict-AND boolean). Both conjuncts must hold for F1 to fire; one alone is insufficient to reject the wealth-bias mechanism. If F1 fires, we reframe the paper's headline as a universal income effect under separability, abandoning the wealth-bias hypothesis.

\item[\textbf{F2 (informative, not rejecting).}] Reports on the supervision-cost channel \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}. F2 fires iff $p(\hat\beta_4 \ne 0) > .10$ at all three bandwidths (T1, T2, T3). The EK-1 sign is theoretically indeterminate ex ante because the transfer relaxes both the family-time scarcity (favoring more off-farm labor) and the cash constraint that drives supervised hiring (favoring less off-farm labor as own-farm activity expands). A null $\hat\beta_4$ is therefore consistent with either (i) the supervision-cost wedge being slack at this scale or (ii) the two opposing effects exactly canceling within the SFFP eligibility window. F2 is reported as supporting evidence on the labor-imperfection margin; F2 alone neither rejects nor confirms the AHM-separability test.

\item[\textbf{Joint reading.}] Three exhaustive outcomes:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{F1 does not fire $+$ F2 fires (modal):} the wealth-biased liquidity channel is supported and the supervision channel is not detected at SFFP scale. The headline AHM-separability rejection rests on F1.
\item \emph{F1 does not fire $+$ F2 does not fire:} both channels are operative. Strongest reading; AHM-separability rejected via two independent margins.
\item \emph{F1 fires (regardless of F2):} the wealth-bias-channel signature is absent. The paper repositions as a precise null estimate---a developed-country complement to the demographic-instrument null estimates in \citet{LaFaveThomas2016_markets}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{description}

\paragraph{Equilibrium rent caveat (B.1 disclosure).} The Korean data exhibit a rent\_cost pass-through of approximately $-12.0\%$ at T2 (\S\ref{sec:robustness} CH4 channel-decomposition table)---a sign opposite to the positive capitalization estimates in \citet{Kirwan2009_rentcap} for the U.S.\ and \citet{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap} for the EU. These observed rent movements are \emph{consistent with} but \emph{not derived from} our AHM-extension model. The micro mechanism is: rented-area contraction at the household level (an implication of $\partial A_{own}/\partial T_{SFFP} > 0$ from \ref{eq:CO-1} via substitution from rented to owned land) aggregates into a reduced rental-demand schedule. Standard incidence theory \citep{Floyd1965_incidence, AlstonJames2002_incidence} then maps the reduced rental-demand to a negative equilibrium-rent response. We report $\hat\beta_5$ as reduced-form evidence consistent with the model's empirical footprint, not as a primary theoretical contribution; a full equilibrium treatment of the rental market is outside the scope of this paper.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

We use Statistics Korea's Farm Household Economic Survey (FHES) Wave 1 panel, 2018--2022. The full panel comprises $14{,}474$ farm-years across $3{,}614$ farms. For the headline DiD-RD estimation we apply \emph{symmetric observable-eligibility screening}: both treated and control households are required to satisfy the three FHES-observable SFFP eligibility criteria except the cutoff itself, namely (ii) household-aggregate owned farmland $< 1.55$~ha and (vi) household off-farm income $< 45$~M~KRW. Households failing either criterion are dropped from \emph{both} sides of the cutoff. The resulting analysis sample is $N = 11{,}010$ farm-years across $2{,}776$ farms with treatment-group share at the household level $\bar D = 0.407$ (treatment status is fixed by the 2018 baseline area and is time-invariant within household; the farm-year proportion of treated observations, $0.388$, differs slightly due to differential attrition).\footnote{The FHES panel is administered by Statistics Korea and accessed under restricted-microdata terms through the Microdata Integrated Service (MDIS), \url{https://mdis.kostat.go.kr/}. Full-panel statistics ($14{,}474$ farm-years, $3{,}614$ farms) and symmetric-screened analysis statistics ($11{,}010$ farm-years, $2{,}776$ farms, household-level $\bar D = 0.407$) are derived from the author's internal verification on \texttt{panel\_2018\_2022.dta} (log dated 2026-05-06) and the symmetric-construction script \texttt{scripts/R/01d\_symmetric\_clean.R} (log dated 2026-05-20); both are mirrored in the replication package. Referees and replicators can request equivalent panel construction via MDIS application; the synthetic-generator path (\texttt{scripts/R/synthetic/}, AEA DCAS v1.0 code-only verification) preserves identification-relevant covariate moments and cutoff density without disclosing restricted microdata.}

\paragraph{Panel-attrition decomposition.} Within the symmetric-screened sample of $2{,}776$ farms $\times\,5$ years $= 13{,}880$ expected farm-years, $11{,}010$ are observed ($79.3\%$); the residual is missing due to a combination of (i) rolling-panel entry post-2018, (ii) farms exiting before 2022 (mortality, retirement, household dissolution), and (iii) sporadic survey non-response. We test the null of equal attrition across the eligibility cutoff via a placebo regression with the attrition indicator as outcome and the treatment dummy $D_i$ as predictor, reporting the differential-attrition estimate in \S\ref{sec:robustness} alongside the \citet{McCrary2008_density} cutoff-density continuity test. The frozen 2018-baseline running variable ensures that attrition realized in 2019--2022 cannot retroactively alter eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area; the remaining concern is whether observed attrition is differential across the cutoff, which the placebo regression addresses.

\paragraph{Variable construction.} We follow the FHES naming convention (operationalized in the replication-package R conventions §10). The running variable $rv_{2018,i} = A_{2018,i} - 5{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2} is the 2018-baseline cultivated area centered at the 0.5~ha cutoff (Table~\ref{tab:notation}). The treatment dummy $D_i$ is the statutorily eligible indicator, defined as the conjunction of the three FHES-observable SFFP eligibility criteria (\S\ref{sec:institutional-eligibility}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Di-def}
D_i = \mathbf{1}\{rv_{2018,i} \le 0\} \cdot \mathbf{1}\{A_{owned,2018,i} < 15{,}500 \text{ m}^2\} \cdot \mathbf{1}\{Y_{off,2018,i} < 45{,}000{,}000 \text{ KRW}\},
\end{equation}
all evaluated at the 2018 baseline.

We apply the same observable-eligibility screening on both sides of the cutoff: households failing criteria (ii) or (vi) are dropped from both treated and control groups, ensuring symmetry of the analysis sample in observable eligibility around the 0.5~ha cutoff. On the resulting sample of $2{,}776$ farms, $D_i = 1 \iff rv_{2018,i} \le 0$ in the three FHES-observable dimensions; the design identifies an intent-to-treat (ITT) effect on the screened subpopulation. The estimand is NOT a sharp ATT because the unobservable SFFP criteria (iii)--(v), (vii), (viii) are administratively enforced and not present in the survey data (\S\ref{sec:institutional-eligibility}); the ITT integrates over administrative non-receipt within the screened-eligible population. The 2018-baseline freeze rules out post-period manipulation of eligibility status. The post-period indicator $\text{Post}_t = \mathbf{1}\{\text{year} \ge 2020\}$ switches on at the 2020-05-01 PIDPS effective date. The asymmetric-sample variant of this design (Wave 7 baseline, dropping only the 194 treated-but-ineligible households without screening the control side) is reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness-asymmetric} as a comparator.

Primary outcomes are $A_{own,i,t}$ (own-cultivated area, m\textsuperscript{2}; primary \#1) and $\text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}_{i,t}$ (operating cost net of rent, KRW; primary \#2, the rent-net signal isolates the lumpy-capital response from the Kirwan pass-through channel). The full operating cost $\text{op\_cost}_{i,t} = \text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}_{i,t} + \text{rent\_cost}_{i,t}$ is reported alongside as the rent-inclusive omnibus comparator. Auxiliary and ex-theory outcomes are $\text{off\_farm\_income}_{i,t}$ (F2 supervision-channel diagnostic) and $\text{unit\_rent\_price}_{i,t}$ (aggregate-equilibrium implication per \S\ref{sec:predictions} caveat). The baseline tenancy ratio $s_{0,i} = A_{own,2018,i}/A_{2018,i}$ is constructed once from 2018 reported areas and used uniformly across the five-bin partition (Online Appendix \S B.1).

\paragraph{Sample restrictions and descriptive statistics.} The analysis sample comprises $11{,}010$ farm-years across $2{,}776$ farms after symmetric observable-eligibility screening of the FHES Wave 1 rolling panel, 2018--2022 (\S\ref{sec:institutional-identification}). Table~\ref{tab:descriptives_en} reports population-weighted means of the running variable, primary outcomes, and key covariates by treatment status. \section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:identification}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Design overview.} We exploit the 0.5~ha SFFP eligibility cutoff via a difference-in-differences regression-discontinuity (DiD-RD) design. The identification logic combines the spatial discontinuity at $rv_{2018,i} = 0$ (eligibility margin) with the temporal discontinuity at $\text{Post}_t = 1$ (PIDPS effective date, 2020-05-01). The 2018-baseline freeze of $rv_{2018,i}$ rules out post-policy manipulation of eligibility status, as discussed in \S\ref{sec:ahm-baseline} (\textit{Estimand and pre-period inference}); the McCrary continuity test (\S\ref{sec:robustness}) further verifies absence of pre-2018 strategic area reporting in anticipation of the policy.

\paragraph{Specification.} We estimate the DiD-RD specification
\begin{equation}
Y_{k,i,t} \;=\; \alpha_k + \beta_k\, D_i \cdot \text{Post}_t + \gamma_k\, D_i + \zeta_k\, \text{Post}_t + f_k(rv_{2018,i}) + \mathbf{x}_{i,t}'\boldsymbol\theta_k + \varepsilon_{k,i,t}
\label{eq:didrd-main}
\end{equation}
for each of the four primary outcomes $Y_{k,i,t}$ indexed by $k \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$: own-cultivated area ($A_{\text{own}}$, primary \#1), operating cost net of rent (primary \#2), off-farm income (auxiliary, F2 supervision-channel diagnostic), and unit rent price (ex-theory, aggregate-equilibrium implication). The function $f_k(rv_{2018,i})$ is a local-linear polynomial in the running variable with a triangular kernel within the bandwidth, $\mathbf{x}_{i,t}$ is a vector of pre-period covariates (farm-type indicators, head-of-household age tier, and education tier), and $\beta_k$ is the DiD-RD coefficient on outcome $k$. The estimand corresponds to an intent-to-treat (ITT) over eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area; see \S\ref{sec:ahm-baseline} for the LATE-at-cutoff interpretation.

\paragraph{Bandwidth grid.} We report three bandwidths in parallel rather than a single optimal-bandwidth point estimate: T1 ($h = 500$~m\textsuperscript{2}, the marginal-window spec emphasizing households with $A_{2018,i}$ near 0.5~ha), T2 ($h = 1{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2}, the working specification balancing precision and locality), and T3 (MSE-optimal $h$ via \citealp{CalonicoCattaneoTitiunik2014_rdrobust} with a triangular kernel). The T1/T2/T3 grid surfaces the spatial concentration of the effect and pre-empts the bandwidth-cherry-picking concern.

\paragraph{Heterogeneity / F1.} For the load-bearing wealth-bias channel (\S\ref{sec:liquidity}), we estimate (\ref{eq:didrd-main}) separately within each of the five baseline-tenancy bins $b(i) \in \{$\text{pure tenant}, \text{low\_owner}, \text{mixed}, \text{high\_owner}, \text{pure\_owner}$\}$ (partition fixed at 2018 baseline; see Online Appendix \S B.1 for derivation). F1 fires (the wealth-bias mechanism is rejected) iff both of the following conditions hold: (i) the bin-1 pure-tenant coefficient $\hat\beta_{1,\text{tenant}}$ is statistically zero at T2 ($p > .10$); and (ii) the five-bin point estimates fail to be weakly monotone in $(1 - s_{0,i})$ at T2. The symmetric-screened estimation delivers $\hat\beta_{1,\text{tenant}} = +1{,}151$~m\textsuperscript{2} ($p = .036$, T2) with the monotone gradient confirmed across the four non-pure-owner bins; the first conjunct of the F1 trigger fails, so F1 does not fire (\S\ref{sec:results}). The asymmetric-sample variant (Wave 7 baseline; $+1{,}122$, $p = .041$) reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness-asymmetric} preserves the sign and ordering.

\paragraph{Inference.} Standard errors are cluster-robust at the household level ($hh\_id$, $2{,}776$ clusters); wild cluster bootstrap p-values follow \citet{CameronGelbachMiller2008_clusterboot} with $B = 9{,}999$ Rademacher refits per cell. Across the primary outcome family (the four outcomes: $A_{\text{own}}$, $\text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}$, $\text{off\_farm\_income}$, and $\text{consumption}$ or $\text{farm\_income}$ as the omnibus), we apply Holm step-down correction to control the family-wise error rate. Province-by-year fixed-effect absorption (\texttt{sido\_cd}~$\times$~\texttt{year}) is reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness} (Table~\ref{tab:sido_robustness_en}) as a robustness check addressing within-province rental-market spillovers. Sub-district ($sgg\_cd$) geography is not released by Statistics Korea MDIS for FHES Wave 1; the 17-province sido grouping is the finest geographic stratifier we can use, of which 16 sido contain symmetric-screened households (Seoul is empty by construction).

\paragraph{Identifying assumptions.} The closed-form predictions in \S\ref{sec:liquidity}--\S\ref{sec:supervision} rest on the regularity conditions C1--C5 stated in Online Appendix \S B.1. The 2018-baseline freeze, McCrary density continuity, and differential-attrition placebo (\S\ref{sec:robustness}) jointly underwrite the DiD-RD identification.

\paragraph{SUTVA and equilibrium spillover.} The rent-cost pass-through reported in \S\ref{sec:results} ($-13.7\%$ at T1, $-11.7\%$ at T2) is the aggregate-equilibrium implication of the household-level F1 pivot: when statutorily eligible smallholders substitute from rented to owned land, the rental-demand schedule contracts and the equilibrium rental rate falls. This is internally consistent with the §\ref{sec:predictions} derivation of $\hat\beta_5$ as an ex-theory aggregate-equilibrium object, but it raises a SUTVA concern for the F1 identification itself: the same rent fall that produces $\hat\beta_5 < 0$ feeds back through the rent-vs-own indifference condition (\ref{eq:CO-threshold}), so control-side households (whose $A_{2018,i} > 0.5$~ha leaves them ineligible) also face a lower equilibrium rent and may shift toward renting more, partially absorbing the cross-cutoff comparison. We address this concern as follows. First, we re-estimate (\ref{eq:didrd-main}) replacing the scalar year fixed effect with province-by-year fixed effects ($\texttt{sido\_cd} \times \texttt{year}$), absorbing province-specific rental-market clearing. Table~\ref{tab:sido_robustness_en} reports the 24 cells (4 outcomes $\times$ 3 bandwidths $\times$ 2 specs): 0 sign flips on the shared outcomes vs.\ the scalar-FE main specification, median magnitude shift $12\%$, F1 monotone-gradient claim unaffected. Second, Korean rural-rental markets clear at the village or sub-district level---finer than \texttt{sido\_cd}---but FHES Wave 1 does not release sub-district geography (MDIS publishes \texttt{sido\_cd} only). Provincial absorption is the conservative substitute available. Third, the sign of the residual within-province bias is ambiguous in theory: a lower equilibrium rent makes renting cheaper for control households (pushing their own-area pivot negative) but also reduces the treated households' marginal cost of owning relative to renting (partially offsetting the SFFP-induced substitution). A directional bias argument is not pursued here. Instead, Table~\ref{tab:sido_robustness_en} provides the operative empirical bound: the median absolute coefficient shift under province-by-year FE absorption is $12\%$, and no sign flips occur on the shared outcomes. The F1 monotone-gradient claim survives this empirical shift. Outcomes touching the rental margin ($\hat\beta_5$, $\text{rent\_cost}$, $\text{area\_rent}$) remain reduced-form aggregate-equilibrium objects and are not interpreted as partial-equilibrium identification (\S\ref{sec:predictions} ¶3 equilibrium-rent caveat).

\paragraph{Cutoff-density continuity (McCrary 2008).} We verify continuity of the running-variable density at the 0.5~ha cutoff via the \citet{McCrary2008_density} density-discontinuity test on the 2018 baseline cross-section of the symmetric-screened sample. The full-sample test statistic is $t = 0.68$ ($p = .50$, $n = 2{,}178$); the null of density continuity is not rejected. Narrow-window tests at $\pm 500$ and $\pm 1{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2} return $p = .064$ and $p = .076$ respectively---approaching but not reaching the $\alpha = .05$ threshold and consistent with noisy local-window estimation rather than a manipulation signature; the wider $\pm 3{,}300$~m\textsuperscript{2} window returns $p = .36$. Figure~\ref{fig:mccrary} visualizes the density on either side of the cutoff. The frozen 2018-baseline construction of $rv_{2018,i}$ rules out endogenous post-period sorting by design; the McCrary continuity test addresses the residual pre-2018 strategic-reporting concern flagged in \S\ref{sec:ahm-baseline} (\textit{Estimand and pre-period inference}). The symmetric observable-eligibility screening (\S\ref{sec:identification}) eliminates households above the (ii) and (vi) thresholds on both sides of the cutoff by construction; consequently the triple-margin McCrary extension reported in earlier draft versions is mechanical on the symmetric sample (the density on the upper side of the owned-area and off-farm-income thresholds is zero), and we report only the area-margin test here. The narrow-window McCrary statistics are corroborated by the modern Cattaneo-Jansson-Ma (2020) \texttt{rddensity} jackknife test (Table~\ref{tab:cjm_density_en}): full-sample $t_{jk} = 0.68$ ($p = .50$), narrow windows return $p \in [.06, .36]$ in the same pattern. CJM's bandwidth-robust inference confirms the McCrary reading.

\paragraph{HonestDiD sensitivity.} The DiD-RD parallel-trends gate relies on a single pre-period (2018$\to$2019); per \citet{Roth2022_pretrends}, single-pre-period pretests do not certify the parallel-trends restriction. We report \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid} sensitivity bounds for the event-study post-period coefficients on $A_{\text{own}}$ and op\_cost\_ex\_rent using their relative-magnitudes approach with $\bar M \in \{0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2\}$, one pre-period (2018), and three post-periods (2020--2022). Figure~\ref{fig:honestdid} visualizes the sensitivity curve. The event-study post-period coefficients are individually small ($A_{\text{own}}$ at 2022: $+225$~m\textsuperscript{2} with CI $[-242, +331]$ at $\bar M = 0$); the 95\% confidence interval includes zero already at $\bar M = 0$ --- i.e., the HonestDiD breakdown $\bar M^* = 0$.

$\bar M^* = 0$ is the binding interpretation: the event-study identification of $\hat\beta_1$ at a single post-year-by-bandwidth cell cannot tolerate any linear-trend deviation while preserving the post-period coefficient sign. The HonestDiD result is therefore not evidence supporting the headline; the headline rests on identification anchors that the sensitivity test does not adjudicate. Specifically, the load-bearing F1 evidence is the \emph{cross-bin gradient} on $A_{\text{own}}$ (pure-tenant $+1{,}151$, low-owner $+438$, both $p < .05$ at T2; monotone-decreasing through mixed and high-owner bins to the pure-owner zero anchor). The cross-bin gradient is identified off heterogeneity across $s_{0,i}$ within the post-period, not off the magnitude of any single bin's post-period coefficient; the gradient cannot be mimicked by a universal trend deviation common to all bins. A bin-specific HonestDiD analysis would directly bound the bin-heterogeneity differential against bin-specific linear trends and is the appropriate next robustness exercise, alongside the FHES Wave 2 panel extension (post-2023); both are deferred to follow-up work. The 2018 placebo coefficients on $A_{\text{own}}$ across the four non-pure-owner bins are individually small ($|t| < 1$ at each bin), consistent with parallel pre-trends in the available pre-period. The $\bar M^* = 0$ result is therefore a property of the single-pre-period pooled event-study spec rather than evidence against the F1 monotone-gradient claim.

\paragraph{Placebo cutoffs.}\label{sec:robustness-placebo} We test for spurious discontinuities by re-estimating the DiD-RD at four non-policy cutoffs (0.3, 0.4, 0.6, 0.7~ha) using the same Spec A specification (\ref{eq:didrd-main}) on the analysis sample. The marginally-significant placebo cells (farm\_income at 0.3~ha T1; consumption at 0.4~ha T2; op\_cost at 0.6~ha T2) are scattered across different outcomes and cutoffs and do not survive a Bonferroni correction across the 32 tests. The true-cutoff (0.5~ha) results in \S\ref{sec:results} remain the unique signature; we read this as evidence against a spurious-cutoff explanation for the headline.

\paragraph{Why area-only as robustness.}\label{sec:robustness-areaonly} The original baseline (pre-Wave-7) of this analysis used the area-only treatment definition $D^{\text{area}}_i = \mathbf{1}\{A_{2018,i} \le 5{,}000 \text{ m}^2\}$, reflecting only SFFP criterion (i) and ignoring the observable owned-area (ii) and off-farm-income (vi) criteria. We promoted the three-criterion conjunction $D_i$ (eq.~\ref{eq:Di-def}) to the main analysis to align the empirical treatment indicator with the statutory SFFP eligibility definition (\S\ref{sec:institutional-eligibility}), not because the area-only finding was empirically weaker. We retain the area-only definition as a robustness check for readers who interpret the 0.5~ha cutoff as the institutional gate and treat the additional (ii)+(vi) criteria as auxiliary; the area-only design is also closer to a textbook sharp RD at a single threshold without the second-criterion conditioning.

\paragraph{Area-only treatment robustness.} The 24 journal-cell coefficient comparison on the full panel (without symmetric screening; see \texttt{treatment\_definition\_comparison.md} in the replication package) under the area-only definition shows the F1 monotone tenancy gradient is preserved in direction and ordering across the four non-pure-owner bins, and the operating-cost-net-of-rent point estimate remains negative at T1 ($-4.02$M~KRW, $p = .055$) and T2 ($-3.13$M, $p = .097$). The headline conclusions are not driven by the treatment-definition choice. Figure~\ref{fig:forest-treatment-defs} visualizes the side-by-side comparison across the 24 cells, comparing the area-only baseline to the symmetric-screened main estimates: 1 sign flip on the off-farm-income outcome (which is statistically near zero under both designs), 3 cells crossing $\alpha = .05$ on direction-preserving outcomes, and 10 cells crossing $\alpha = .10$, with median absolute percentage shift of $39\%$. The shift is larger than the previously-reported Wave 7 comparison (asymmetric-sample vs.\ area-only, median $\approx 16\%$) because the symmetric screening drops 644 control-side households failing criteria (ii) or (vi); their re-inclusion under the area-only baseline expands the comparator pool. The qualitative reading is unchanged: per-farm flat-rate eligibility produces the same F1 monotone-gradient signature regardless of whether the secondary criteria are applied as additional treatment restrictions.

\paragraph{Asymmetric-sample variant (Wave 7 baseline).}\label{sec:robustness-asymmetric} A natural intermediate between the area-only design and the symmetric-screened main design is the asymmetric-sample variant: drop the 194 treated-but-ineligible households (failing (ii) or (vi) on the treated side) but leave the control side unscreened. We label this the asymmetric-sample variant because the screening is one-sided. Re-estimating the F1 monotone gradient on the asymmetric sample (3,420 farms / 13,689 farm-years) produces a pure-tenant T2 coefficient of $+1{,}122$~m\textsuperscript{2} ($p = .041$) and a four-bin gradient $+1{,}122 \to +403 \to +222 \to -74 \to 0$ --- qualitatively identical to the symmetric main result but with a slightly less tight monotone ordering. The operating-cost-net-of-rent T1 estimate on the asymmetric sample is $-3.98$M~KRW ($p = .057$). The asymmetric sample's treated-side screening yields the same identification advantage of within-cutoff observable-eligibility comparability, while the control side retains the broader population of large-farm landlords and off-farm-income-rich households; results are directionally identical to the symmetric main. The asymmetric-sample variant is the cleaner "sample-size-maximized" version of the design (3,420 vs.\ 2,776 farms), but it does not yield two-sided observable-eligibility symmetry because eligibility-conditioning is one-sided.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Policy implications.} The SFFP's per-farm flat-rate design induces a substantively meaningful land-tenure shift among the credit-constrained non-pure-owner smallholder subpopulation at the 0.5~ha cutoff. The pure-tenant own-cultivated-area response of $+1{,}151$~m\textsuperscript{2} at T2 is approximately 23\% of the 0.5~ha eligibility threshold, and the same response monotonically attenuates across the four non-pure-owner tenancy bins ($+1{,}151 \to +438 \to +258 \to -52 \to 0$). The pattern is associated with a relaxed rental--ownership margin among households with low baseline ownership share, consistent with the wealth-biased liquidity mechanism. The operating-cost-net-of-rent response at narrow bandwidth ($-3.57$~M~KRW at T1) is supportive of the $(S,s)$ inaction prediction in (\ref{eq:CO-3}) rather than a load-bearing test: at $T_{SFFP}/\tau \in [0.024, 0.048]$, the transfer is too small to trigger a discrete capital-adjustment crossing, and households absorb the marginal subsidy on variable-cost margins rather than expanding lumpy capital stock.

\paragraph{Incidence-reversal contrast with Kirwan (2009) and Baldoni-Ciaian (2023).} Our reduced-form rent-cost pass-through of $-13.7\%$ at T1 ($p < .10$) and $-11.7\%$ at T2 reverses the $+25\%$ landlord rent-capitalization observed by \citet{Kirwan2009_rentcap} for U.S.\ Farm Service Agency direct payments and the $+46$--$55\%$ short-run / long-run pass-through reported by \citet{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap} for EU CAP Pillar I. The mechanism behind the incidence reversal is the per-farm flat-rate design itself: SFFP eligibility is a binary household-level treatment, not a marginal per-hectare payment that landlords can extract through rent renegotiation. The negative reduced-form $\hat\beta_5$ is the aggregate-equilibrium implication of the household-level rented-area substitution toward ownership: the household-model core does not predict $\hat\beta_5$ directly but produces the underlying area-substitution that aggregates into the observed rent-market response. Standard incidence theory \citep{Floyd1965_incidence, AlstonJames2002_incidence} maps the reduced rental-demand schedule (which contracts as eligible smallholders substitute from rented to owned land) to a negative equilibrium-rent response; we report $\hat\beta_5$ as reduced-form evidence consistent with the model's empirical footprint, not as a primary theoretical contribution.

\paragraph{Korean smallholder context.} The credit-constrained smallholder cohort observed at the 0.5~ha cutoff is dominated by an aging operator population (mean head-of-household age $\approx 67$; cf.\ replication-package descriptives table), paddy-rice production ($\approx 85\%$ of cultivated area), and a $52\%$ pure-owner share among treated households (the wealth-bias mechanism operates on the $48\%$ non-pure-owner margin per the F1 four-bin gradient). The $92.3\%$ administrative-receipt rate among eligible households reflects high policy salience; the imputation-driven $100\%$ ITT coverage in our DiD-RD design is the appropriate identification target for the AHM-extension test. Whether the result extends to other small-farm direct-payment designs that sever the per-hectare landlord-capture channel is an open question; the broader Asian-smallholder literature on per-farm flat-rate transfers could productively consider this incidence-reversal as a design-driven possibility, subject to separate identification.

\paragraph{Complement to demographic-instrument separability tests.} The separability-test literature \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} has typically rejected separability in developing-country settings (e.g., Indonesia, India) using demographic shocks (household composition) as instruments for the structural test. Our setting bridges this literature in two directions. First, we reject separability in a developed-country setting (Korea, OECD high-income economy), demonstrating that the AHM-separability null can fail even where credit and labor markets are nominally well-developed. Second, we use a sharp policy cutoff for identification rather than demographic instruments, eliminating the typical concerns about instrument-exclusion-restriction violations and household-composition endogeneity. The shared substantive reading---separability fails when credit, labor, or land markets are incomplete at the smallholder margin---is robust across the two identification strategies.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Three caveats temper the headline. (i) The DiD-RD design uses a single pre-period (2018$\to$2019), which limits parallel-trends inference per \citet{Roth2022_pretrends}; we report \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid} $\bar M$ sensitivity bounds (\S\ref{sec:robustness}) to quantify the linear-trend-bias robustness. (ii) The FHES Wave 1 panel restricts the analysis window to 2018--2022; Wave 2 microdata linkage (post-2023) is forthcoming and will permit a longer-run dynamic extension of the Carter-Olinto threshold rule. (iii) The F2 supervision-channel coefficient $\hat\beta_4$ is statistically near zero across bandwidths, but the sign-indeterminacy of the EK derivation (\S\ref{sec:supervision}) prevents a clean interpretation: the null is observationally equivalent to either the supervision wedge being slack at SFFP scale or to opposing family-time and cash-constraint effects exactly canceling. The 0.5~ha cutoff is institutionally unique; external validity to other Korean policy thresholds or other Asian smallholder contexts requires separate study---a one-line reference to alternative non-separability literatures (\citealp{BanerjeeGertlerGhatak2002_tenancy} on tenancy-contract design; \citealp{KhanThomas2008_idiosyncratic} on macro-investment heterogeneity) is offered as out-of-scope but related comparators.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}\label{sec:conclusion}
We test the Agricultural Household Model's separability null at the 0.5~ha eligibility cutoff for Korea's per-farm flat-rate Small-Farmer Flat Payment, and we reject it for the credit-constrained non-pure-owner subpopulation via the wealth-biased liquidity channel of \citet{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}. The pure-tenant own-cultivated-area response of $+1{,}151$~m\textsuperscript{2} at T2 ($p = .036$) and the monotone four-bin tenancy gradient ($1{,}151 \to 438 \to 258 \to -52 \to 0$ across pure\_tenant, low\_owner, mixed, high\_owner, pure\_owner) uniquely identify the wealth-bias signature, ruling out a universal income-effect channel under which all subgroups would respond proportionally. The negative operating-cost-net-of-rent response of $-3.57$~M~KRW at T1 supports the $(S,s)$ inaction reading at the calibrated $T_{SFFP}/\tau \in [0.024, 0.048]$, though it is not individually significant. The $-13.7\%$ rent-cost pass-through at T1 ($p < .10$) is reported as the aggregate-equilibrium implication of the household-level area-substitution toward ownership; it reverses the $+25\%$--$55\%$ landlord rent-capitalization observed under area-proportional U.S.\ and EU CAP designs and is consistent with the per-farm flat-rate mechanism severing the landlord-capture channel.

The 0.5~ha SFFP cutoff matters as an identification instrument: a frozen 2018-baseline running variable rules out post-policy manipulation by construction, and the per-farm flat-rate design eliminates landlord rent extraction---a confound that demographic-instrument approaches \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} cannot cleanly separate. The result generalizes the separability-test literature to a developed-country smallholder setting with discontinuity-based ITT identification on the symmetric-screened subpopulation, and the four-bin tenancy gradient provides a structurally interpretable signature that the universal-income-effect alternative cannot match.

Four directions remain open for future work. (i) The off-farm-income supervision-channel coefficient $\hat\beta_4$ is near zero in our specification; richer Korean labor-market data---particularly the FHES Wave 2 linkage scheduled for post-2023 release---would permit a sharper test of the Eswaran-Kotwal (1986) supervision-cost mechanism by separating the cancellation-regime alternative from the slack-wedge alternative. (ii) The Carter-Olinto threshold rule is currently a static two-period derivation; a multi-period dynamic extension---tracking asset accumulation and tenure transitions over the 2020--2030 horizon---would generate predictions for the medium-run land-tenure trajectory induced by SFFP. (iii) A paper/ko translation targeting the Korean Agricultural Economics Association (KAEA, \textit{Hanguk Nong-gyeongje Hakhoe}) and the KAEA-affiliated journals is queued; the Korean draft will serve as the basis for a thesis-chapter version. (iv) If Korea introduces a second per-farm flat-rate eligibility threshold (e.g., at 1.0~ha to expand the SFFP-eligible population), a cross-cutoff replication would identify the channel-relative-magnitudes more precisely and test whether the wealth-bias signature persists at a different smallholder scale.
