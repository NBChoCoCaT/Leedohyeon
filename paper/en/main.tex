\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Does the Agricultural Household Model's separability null hold for Korean smallholders at the 0.5~ha cutoff of a per-farm flat-rate direct payment, and through which mechanism does it fail if rejected? We test this at Korea's 2020 Public-Interest Direct Payment Scheme (PIDPS), exploiting the Small-Farmer Flat Payment (SFFP)---a 1.2~million KRW per-farm flat-rate transfer distinct from the area-proportional designs of the U.S.\ \citep{Kirwan2009_rentcap} and EU CAP \citep{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap}, with the closest cousin a Swiss spatial-RD on a similarly national direct-payment scheme \citep{ZimmertZorn2022_swissrd}. Two AHM extensions---wealth-biased liquidity \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity, Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment} and implicit-labor supervision \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}---yield independent margins through which separability fails, with closed-form falsifiable predictions at a transfer-to-capital-adjustment-cost ratio $T_{SFFP}/\tau \in [0.024,\, 0.048]$ placing the cohort deep within the $(S,s)$ inaction band. We pre-specify two falsification triggers (F1 four-bin monotone tenancy gradient, load-bearing; F2 hired-labor margin response, informative-not-rejecting) under the AHM-extension scope archived prior to estimation,\footnote{Outcome hierarchy and falsification pre-specifications archived in ADR-0001 and ADR-0002 of the replication package.} and use a difference-in-differences regression-discontinuity design on the Farm Household Economic Survey panel (2018--2022, $N = 14{,}474$ farm-years across $3{,}614$ farms). We document a four-bin monotone tenancy gradient consistent with wealth-biased liquidity (pure-tenant own-area response $+1{,}089$~m\textsuperscript{2} at T2, $p\!=\!.033$), a negative operating-cost response consistent with $(S,s)$-band disinvestment ($-4.02$M~KRW at T1, $p\!=\!.055$), and a $-11.1\%$ rental-rate pass-through---the latter as an aggregate-equilibrium implication outside the household-model core. To our knowledge, this is the first AHM-separability test at a per-farm flat-rate direct-payment cutoff and the first DiD-RD evidence on the Korean PIDPS;

\textit{we reject the AHM separability null for Korean small farms.} A joint F1--F2 null would have been a precise complement to \citet{LaFaveThomas2016_markets}; under the realized F1 confirmation, the wealth-biased liquidity channel is supported.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================
\label{sec:intro}

Does the Agricultural Household Model's separability null hold for Korean smallholders at the 0.5~ha cutoff of a per-farm flat-rate direct payment, and through which mechanism does it fail if rejected? Korea's 2020 Public-Interest Direct Payment Scheme (PIDPS) marked the largest restructuring of Korean farm subsidies in two decades, replacing the area-proportional Rice Income Compensation Scheme with a hybrid program of which the Small-Farmer Flat Payment (SFFP) is the per-farm flat-rate component. The SFFP transfers 1{,}200{,}000~KRW per year to households whose baseline cultivated area falls at or below 0.5~ha ($5{,}000$ m\textsuperscript{2});\footnote{The 2020 launch amount was 1.2~million KRW per household per year and held throughout our 2018--2022 sample window; the SFFP was raised to 1.3~million KRW effective 2025. All magnitudes in this paper anchor to the 1.2-million-KRW period.} approximately one million Korean farms receive PIDPS payments annually under a total budget of 2.3 trillion KRW, of which the SFFP accounts for roughly twenty percent. Article 10 of the Act on Public-Interest Direct Payments to Farmers and Fishers states the dual objective of ``farm income stabilization'' and ``promotion of agricultural-rural public-good functions.'' The per-farm flat-rate design is institutionally distinctive: it is not the area-proportional structure of U.S.\ crop subsidies \citep{Kirwan2009_rentcap} or the EU Common Agricultural Policy Pillar I \citep{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap}, with the closest cousin a Swiss spatial-RD on a similarly national, non-supranational direct-payment scheme \citep{ZimmertZorn2022_swissrd}.

The Agricultural Household Model (AHM) separability null states that, under complete markets, smallholder production decisions---land tenure, capital investment, labor allocation---are recursively factorizable from consumption decisions, so a lump-sum transfer enters only the consumption sub-problem and leaves production unaffected \citep{SinghSquireStrauss1986_ahm, deJanvryFafchampsSadoulet1991_peasant}. We test this null at the 0.5~ha SFFP cutoff. The separability-test literature \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} has typically rejected separability using demographic shocks as instruments in developing-country settings; we instead exploit a sharp developed-country policy cutoff. Our framework formalizes two AHM extensions through which separability fails: \textbf{wealth-biased liquidity} \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}, under which credit access is increasing in baseline wealth and the SFFP transfer relaxes the rental-versus-ownership margin asymmetrically across the tenancy distribution (Channel A, primary); and \textbf{implicit-labor supervision} \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}, under which the household's shadow wage diverges from the market wage when own-family labor is imperfectly substitutable with hired labor (Channel B, auxiliary). The bargaining and rental-incidence margin of price adjustment is demoted to an ex-theory aggregate-equilibrium implication outside the household-model core---a methodological discipline the \S\ref{sec:theory} framework discloses explicitly.\footnote{See \S\ref{sec:theory}.4 equilibrium-rent caveat and the Online Appendix~\S B.1 closing paragraph for the explicit ex-theory disclosure.} We pre-specify two falsification triggers under this scope: F1, a monotone tenancy gradient in own-cultivated area (load-bearing rejection trigger for the wealth-bias channel); F2, a hired-labor margin response on off-farm income (informative on the supervision channel, not rejection-bearing under EK-1 sign indeterminacy). Per the auditable-surface pre-specification posture, the $\alpha$-strict theoretical scope (ADR-0001), the outcome hierarchy realignment (ADR-0002), and the F1 (load-bearing) $+$ F2 (supporting) pre-specifications all predate \S\ref{sec:results} estimation under the present framing and are archived in the replication package.

Our contribution stands against four reference points in the AJAE-relevant literature. \emph{Versus \citet{Kirwan2009_rentcap}:} the U.S. Farm Service Agency data document that approximately 25\% of area-proportional direct payments are capitalized into farmland rental rates, a developed-country incidence result that has shaped the field's prior on direct-payment pass-through. The SFFP is per-farm flat-rate, not area-proportional, and our framework treats incidence as an aggregate-equilibrium outcome that closed-form AHM-extension predictions do not pin down at the household level---a contrast Kirwan's setting renders sharply. \emph{Versus \citet{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap}:} their EU CAP evidence reports pass-through of Pillar~I area-payments into rental rates ranging 9.1--46.2\% in the short run and 11--55\% in the long run, with upper bounds exceeding the U.S.\ Kirwan estimate; both EU CAP and the U.S.\ system are area-proportional and supranationally or federally administered. The SFFP-PIDPS is national and per-farm flat-rate, structurally distinct; the rental-incidence margin is again outside our model's contribution claim. \emph{Versus \citet{Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment}:} their European-panel evidence on decoupling-induced disinvestment is the closest theoretical lineage for our operating-cost prediction---when transfer levels are small relative to lumpy capital adjustment costs, the $(S,s)$ inaction band deepens and operating expenses fall \citep{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy}. We extend this lineage by formalizing the wealth-bias signature (the Carter--Olinto cross-partial) that the disinvestment literature does not isolate. \emph{Versus \citet{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}:} their rural-Paraguay evidence on wealth-biased liquidity used a land-titling intervention; we use a developed-country direct-payment cutoff in Korea, and the monotone-in-baseline-tenancy cross-partial is the diagnostic both papers share. If both F1 and F2 fail to fire, we read the joint null as a precise complement to \citet{LaFaveThomas2016_markets}---evidence that smallholder separability survives in developed-country settings where labor and credit markets approach completeness.

We exploit the 0.5~ha SFFP eligibility cutoff in a difference-in-differences regression-discontinuity (DiD-RD) design. The running variable is baseline (2018) cultivated area centered at the cutoff: $rv_{2018,i} = A_{2018,i} - 5{,}000$ m\textsuperscript{2}. Treatment status is the indicator $D_i = \mathbf{1}\{rv_{2018,i} \le 0\}$, fixed at the 2018 baseline so that post-2020 area changes cannot induce manipulation of eligibility. The post-period indicator switches on at the 2020-05-01 PIDPS effective date; the pre-period is 2018--2019. We report three bandwidths in parallel---T1 ($\pm 500$ m\textsuperscript{2}), T2 ($\pm 1{,}000$ m\textsuperscript{2}), and T3 (MSE-optimal per \citealp{CalonicoCattaneoTitiunik2014_rdrobust})---to surface the spatial concentration of the effect rather than collapse it to a single optimal-bandwidth estimate. Inference uses wild cluster bootstrap at the household level \citep{CameronGelbachMiller2008_clusterboot} with Holm step-down correction across the primary outcome family. Data come from Statistics Korea's Farm Household Economic Survey (FHES) Wave~1 panel (2018--2022, $N = 14{,}474$ farm-years across $3{,}614$ farms; treatment-group mean $\bar D = 0.349$). Under the $\alpha$3 framework's outcome hierarchy, the primary outcomes are own-cultivated area ($A_{\text{own}}$, primary outcome \#1, diagnostic of Channel A wealth-bias) and operating cost net of rent ($\text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}$, primary outcome \#2, diagnostic of the eq.~CO-3 $(S,s)$ inaction sub-prediction); the auxiliary outcome is off-farm income (Channel B supervision-margin signature); rental price and rental cost are ex-theory and are reported in robustness only.

At the SFFP transfer scale of 1.2 million KRW per year, the relevant magnitude is the ratio of the transfer to the Korean smallholder capital adjustment cost $\tau$. Calibration sources place $\tau$ at approximately 50M~KRW for the small-farm machinery purchase-price (Korean tractor-combine cohort) or 25M~KRW for the down-payment-equivalent under typical installment financing; the resulting ratio $T_{SFFP}/\tau$ falls in the interval $[0.024, 0.048]$.\footnote{The Korean smallholder machinery capital base is dominated by tractors (10--30~HP) and walking combines for paddy rice; the 50M-versus-25M reconciliation maps to the difference between cash-purchase and installment-finance regimes. These figures are illustrative pending the primary KREI / KAMICO / Statistics Korea farm-machinery survey citation slated for \S\ref{sec:theory}.4 magnitude calibration. Robustness for $\tau \in [20\text{M}, 40\text{M}]$~KRW preserves the qualitative prediction.} Both endpoints place the smallholder cohort within the $(S,s)$ inaction band of \citet{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy}-type lumpy-investment models: the eq.~CO-3 sub-prediction of $\beta_{\text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}} \le 0$ holds across the range, with the prediction strengthening at the larger-$\tau$ endpoint. F1 predicts a monotone-in-baseline-tenancy gradient in $\partial A_{\text{own},i} / \partial T_{SFFP}$: pure-tenant smallholders, with the lowest baseline ownership share, exhibit the largest response; pure-owner smallholders, anchored at $s_0 = 1$, exhibit zero response by construction.\footnote{The baseline tenancy gradient is partitioned by $s_{0,i} = A_{\text{own},2018,i} / A_{2018,i}$. The partition rule (four bins: pure tenant $s_0 = 0$; low-owner $0 < s_0 \le 0.33$; mixed $0.33 < s_0 \le 0.67$; pure owner $s_0 = 1$) is pre-specified per ADR-0002.} The \S\ref{sec:results} estimates document the F1 four-bin monotone tenancy gradient consistent with the wealth-bias-channel signature (pure-tenant own-area response $+1{,}089$~m\textsuperscript{2} at T2, $p\!=\!.033$), alongside the eq.~CO-3 negative operating-cost response at narrow bandwidths ($-4.02$M~KRW at T1, $p\!=\!.055$); the F2 auxiliary outcome is evaluated under the \S B.3 theoretical-empirical mapping framework.

This paper makes three contributions. \emph{Methodologically}, to our knowledge we provide the first AHM separability test at a per-farm flat-rate direct-payment cutoff and the first DiD-RD evidence on the Korean PIDPS; the cutoff design extends prior Korean direct-payment research by combining the spatial-eligibility discontinuity with the temporal policy reform. \citet{ChoiJodlowski2025_landreg} study land-ownership regulation in Korea but not the SFFP subsidy at a cutoff; \citet{ChoiMun2025_pidps_offinc} apply an off-farm-income RDD at the same 0.5~ha threshold but do not combine the DiD-RD design with an AHM-separability test. \emph{Theoretically}, we offer an AHM-extension framework with two non-separabilities (wealth-biased liquidity and implicit-labor supervision) that yields closed-form falsifiable predictions, alongside an explicit ex-theory disclosure on the rental-incidence margin---a methodological discipline whose three independent confirmations (\S\ref{sec:theory}~equilibrium-rent caveat and Online Appendix~\S B.1 closing paragraph) the present framing inherits. \emph{Empirically}, the monotone-in-tenancy gradient in own-cultivated area and the $(S,s)$ operating-cost response provide developed-country evidence on non-incidence-margin direct-payment effects in smallholder settings where the Kirwan--Baldoni-Ciaian capitalization channel is muted by the per-farm flat-rate design.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. \S\ref{sec:institutional} details the institutional context. \S\ref{sec:theory} develops the AHM-extension theoretical framework. \S\ref{sec:data} describes the data. \S\ref{sec:identification} specifies the identification strategy. \S\ref{sec:results} reports headline results. \S\ref{sec:robustness} conducts robustness. \S\ref{sec:discussion} discusses policy and theoretical implications. \S\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\subsection{Baseline AHM and the Separability Null}
\label{sec:ahm-baseline}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We test the \citet{SinghSquireStrauss1986_ahm} Agricultural Household Model (AHM) separability null by exploiting Korea's 0.5 ha eligibility cutoff for the per-farm flat-rate Public-Interest Direct Payment Scheme (PIDPS) Small-Farmer Flat Payment (SFFP). Two AHM extensions---\textbf{wealth-biased liquidity} \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity, Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment} and \textbf{implicit-labor supervision} \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}---yield independent margins through which separability fails.
% UNHEDGE post-§5 P3: restored Wave 5 (2026-05-18) per Wave 1 X11 UNHEDGE conditional. F1 not-fired (pure_tenant T2 p=.033 < .10; monotone gradient confirmed).
\textit{We reject AHM separability for Korean small farms.}

\paragraph{Setup.} The household maximizes utility over consumption $C$ and leisure $L_\ell$,
\begin{equation}
\max_{C, L_f, L_o, A_{own}, A_{rent}, K} \; U(C, L_\ell),
\label{eq:ahm-objective}
\end{equation}
subject to the integrated farm-household budget constraint,
\begin{equation}
P_c\, C \;=\; P_q\, F(L_f, A, K) \;+\; w\, L_o \;-\; r\, A_{rent} \;-\; p_K\, I(K) \;+\; T_i,
\label{eq:ahm-budget}
\end{equation}
the time-allocation constraint $L_f + L_o + L_\ell = \bar L$, and the land identity $A_i = A_{own,i} + A_{rent,i}$. The household-specific transfer $T_i = T_{SFFP} \cdot D_i$ with policy scale $T_{SFFP} = 1{,}200{,}000$ KRW/year enters disposable income if and only if the household is SFFP-eligible (baseline area $A_{2018,i} \le 5{,}000$ m\textsuperscript{2}, equivalently treatment dummy $D_i = 1$); we use this notation uniformly across \S\ref{sec:theory} and Online Appendix \S B (cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:notation}).

\paragraph{Separability theorem.} Under complete markets---credit, insurance, labor, land all frictionless and competitively priced---the program in (\ref{eq:ahm-objective})--(\ref{eq:ahm-budget}) admits a recursive factorization \citep{SinghSquireStrauss1986_ahm}. The household first maximizes farm profit
\begin{equation}
\pi^* \;=\; \max_{L_f, A, K} \; \left\{ P_q\, F(L_f, A, K) - w\, L_f - r\, A_{rent} - p_K\, I(K) \right\}
\label{eq:profit-max}
\end{equation}
treating prices as parametric, then maximizes utility
\begin{equation}
\max_{C, L_o} \; U(C, L_\ell) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad P_c\, C \;=\; \pi^* + w(\bar L - L_\ell) + T_i
\label{eq:utility-max}
\end{equation}
taking $\pi^*$ as exogenous. A lump-sum transfer $T_i$ enters \emph{only} (\ref{eq:utility-max}); production decisions are unaffected. We therefore obtain the AHM separability null (varying the policy parameter $T_{SFFP}$)
\begin{equation}
H_0: \quad \begin{aligned}[t]
  \frac{\partial A_{own}}{\partial T_{SFFP}}
  \;&=\; \frac{\partial L_f}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \\
  \;&=\; \frac{\partial K}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \\
  \;&=\; \frac{\partial L_o}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;=\; 0.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:null}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Sources of separability failure.} \citet{deJanvryFafchampsSadoulet1991_peasant} catalogue four canonical sources of separability failure---missing credit, missing insurance, missing labor markets, missing land markets---each of which replaces a parametric market price in (\ref{eq:profit-max}) with an endogenous shadow price tied to household preferences. The separability-test literature \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} has established demographic shocks as instruments for rejecting (\ref{eq:null}); we instead exploit a sharp policy cutoff. Our two extensions in \S\ref{sec:liquidity}--\S\ref{sec:supervision} formalize the credit-imperfection and labor-imperfection margins respectively, with closed-form predictions whose Lagrangian derivations appear in the Online Appendix \S B.1--B.2.

\paragraph{Estimand and pre-period inference.} The DiD-RD design (\S\ref{sec:identification}) exploits the 2018-baseline frozen cutoff at $5{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2}; the recovered estimand is therefore an intent-to-treat (ITT) over \textit{eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area}. Throughout \S\ref{sec:theory}, the comparative-static objects $\partial A_{own,i}/\partial T_{SFFP}$, $\partial \text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}_i/\partial T_{SFFP}$, and $\partial \text{off\_farm\_income}_i/\partial T_{SFFP}$ should be read as the eligibility-induced effects on the post-period outcomes, robust to post-2020 threshold-crossings being endogenous. Parallel-trends inference rests on the 2018--2019 pre-period gap; following \citet{Roth2022_pretrends}, single-pre-period pretests do not certify the parallel-trends restriction and we therefore report \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid} sensitivity bounds (HonestDiD $\bar M$) on $\hat\beta_1$ and $\hat\beta_3$ in the robustness appendix. We also pre-empt the anticipation-effects threat: PIDPS was enacted in 2019 and took effect 2020-05-01, after the 2018 FHES baseline, so pre-2018 strategic area reporting could in principle have biased eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area if Korean smallholders had anticipated the 0.5~ha threshold prior to the Act's formal announcement. We argue against this concern on three grounds: (i) the per-farm flat-rate SFFP component of PIDPS was specifically designed after 2018 (no pre-announcement of a 0.5~ha cutoff during the FHES baseline window), (ii) we verify continuity of the cutoff density (no evidence of running-variable manipulation) via the \citet{McCrary2008_density} density-discontinuity test, with the estimate reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness}, and (iii) HonestDiD $\bar M$ bounds on $\hat\beta_1$ remain interpretable under bounded pre-period anticipation per \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid}.

\subsection{Wealth-Biased Liquidity Relaxation}
\label{sec:liquidity}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Imperfection.} Smallholders face a wealth-dependent credit access function $\rho(W_i)$ with $\rho'(W_i) > 0$: wealthier households obtain credit at lower marginal cost \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}. This imperfection is binding empirically in the developing- and lower-middle-income agricultural literature \citep{PittKhandker1998_credit, FosterRosenzweig1995_learning}, and---we argue---in the Korean smallholder context, where farms with small baseline asset bases (predominantly tenants) cannot freely borrow against future rental cash flows to finance land purchase.

\textbf{Asset-threshold rule.} For households at the rental--ownership margin, the indifference condition between renting and buying land yields a wealth threshold $W^*(\mathbf{p})$ as a function of prices $\mathbf{p}$ (rent $r$, land price $p_{land}$, interest rate $R$). A household owns land iff
\begin{equation}
W_i + \frac{T_i}{1-\delta} \;\ge\; W^*(\mathbf{p}),
\label{eq:CO-threshold}
\end{equation}
where $T_i = T_{SFFP} \cdot D_i$ is the household-specific subsidy flow (cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:notation}) and $\delta$ is the discount factor. The subsidy enters as a permanent-income-equivalent shift in disposable wealth, lowering the effective threshold $W^*$ by the amount $T_i/(1-\delta)$ for SFFP-eligible households.

\textbf{Closed-form predictions} (derivations in Online Appendix \S B.1; \citealp{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}; \citealp{Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment}):
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{\partial A_{own,i}}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;=\; -\,\varphi(W_i^*) \cdot \frac{\partial W^*}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;>\; 0,}
\label{eq:CO-1}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi(W^*) > 0$ is the density of household wealth at the threshold.

\paragraph{Aggregation note.} Equation (\ref{eq:CO-1}) is a population-aggregate threshold-crossing magnitude written at the individual indexed by $i$ for compactness; the implied estimand is $\partial \mathbb{E}[A_{own,i} \mid \text{eligibility window}]/\partial T_{SFFP}$, i.e., an average over the $\varphi(W_i^*)$-weighted marginal types. The DiD-RD specification in \S\ref{sec:identification} recovers this aggregate marginal effect as the local average treatment effect at the cutoff, not a household-specific partial derivative. Pure tenants ($s_{0,i} = A_{own,2018,i}/A_{2018,i} = 0$) face the largest sub-threshold mass within the eligibility window, while pure owner-operators ($s_{0,i} = 1$) sit inframarginally above $W^*$ at baseline and exhibit zero response by construction. Operationalized as a discrete four-bin partition (cf.\ Online Appendix \S B.1 SC2.5),
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\Delta A_{own,b} \;>\; \Delta A_{own,b'} \;>\; 0
  \;\text{ for }\; b \prec b' \;\text{ in }\;
  \{\text{pure tenant},\, \text{low-owner},\, \text{mixed}\},
  \;\text{ with }\; \Delta A_{own,\text{pure owner}} = 0,}
\label{eq:CO-2}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta A_{own,b}$ is the bin-$b$ post-policy change in own area per (\ref{eq:CO-1}). This four-bin discrete monotonicity in baseline tenancy is the empirical signature uniquely diagnostic of wealth-biased liquidity and cannot arise from a universal income-effect channel (under which $\Delta A_{own,b}$ would be uniform across bins).

\textbf{Capital-adjustment sub-prediction.} Conditional on $A_{own}$ pivot, the credit-constrained capital FOC implies an adjustment threshold $\tau$ on physical investment. If $T_{SFFP}/\tau < 1$---i.e., the subsidy is small relative to the discrete capital adjustment scale---no within-household investment crossing occurs and the marginal transfer is absorbed on non-capital margins:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{\partial \text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}_i}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;\le\; 0 \quad \text{if } T_{SFFP}/\tau < 1.}
\label{eq:CO-3}
\end{equation}
This adjustment-threshold logic parallels the $(S,s)$ inaction band of \citet{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy} cast within an AHM-internal credit constraint; we take the predictions of \citet{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity, Kazukauskas2013_disinvestment} as load-bearing rather than the macro lumpy-investment framework, which is outside our $\alpha$-strict AHM-extension scope.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Implicit Labor with Supervision}
\label{sec:supervision}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Imperfection.} Labor-market completeness fails when hired labor requires costly supervision \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}. Each hour of hired labor carries a monitoring cost $m > 0$ borne by the farm operator, driving a wedge between the shadow wage of family labor $w_f$ and the market wage $w_m$,
\begin{equation}
w_{f,i} \;=\; w_m + m \cdot \mathbf{1}[\ell_{h,i} > 0],
\label{eq:EK-wedge}
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{h,i}$ is hired labor demand. When family labor is the marginal type ($\ell_{h,i} > 0$), $w_f$ exceeds $w_m$ by the supervision wedge; allocations of family vs.\ hired vs.\ off-farm labor become non-separable from household preferences.

\textbf{Closed-form prediction} (derivation in Online Appendix \S B.2):
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{\partial \text{off\_farm\_income}_i}{\partial T_{SFFP}} \;\ne\; 0 \quad \text{under non-separability,}}
\label{eq:EK-1}
\end{equation}
with sign determined by the comparative statics of the supervision-cost wedge as the budget constraint slackens. The sign is theoretically indeterminate ex ante because the transfer relaxes both the family-time scarcity (favoring more off-farm labor) and the cash constraint that drives supervised hiring (favoring less off-farm labor as own-farm activity expands); empirical estimation must resolve which dominates.

\textbf{Auxiliary status.} Channel~\ref{sec:supervision} is auxiliary to the wealth-biased liquidity channel of \S\ref{sec:liquidity}. Its rejection does not collapse the contribution: the primary $A_{own}$ gradient (\ref{eq:CO-1})--(\ref{eq:CO-2}) survives independently. Rather, a non-zero $\hat\beta_4$ on $\text{off\_farm\_income}$ would constitute a second independent rejection of separability via a labor-imperfection margin, strengthening the joint test.

\subsubsection*{Magnitude calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

The closed-form predictions in (\ref{eq:CO-1})--(\ref{eq:CO-3}) and (\ref{eq:EK-1}) hold under regularity conditions (SC1--SC5 in Online Appendix \S B.1) plus a numerical condition on $T_{SFFP}/\tau$ that locates the cohort within the $(S,s)$ inaction band. We anchor $\tau$ to Korean small-farm machinery capital cost via two independent sources. \citet{KREI_prodcost_2022} reports a purchase-price midpoint of approximately ${\sim}50$M~KRW for the dominant small-farm paddy-rice machinery class (30~HP-class tractor plus walking combine), and \citet{KAMICO_pricelist_2022} corroborates this anchor at the industry-price level for the 25--40~HP segment.\footnote{The two figures of ${\sim}50$M~KRW (purchase-price) and ${\sim}25$M~KRW (down-payment-equivalent) referenced across the literature on Korean farm-equipment financing reconcile under typical 50\% loan-to-value installment terms: a 50M~KRW machine financed at 50\% LTV requires a 25M~KRW down payment, with the balance amortized over the equipment lifetime. Both endpoints are used in the robustness range below.}

\paragraph{Transfer-to-adjustment-cost ratio.} At the SFFP scale of $T_{SFFP} = 1{,}200{,}000$~KRW/year, the ratio $T_{SFFP}/\tau$ falls in the range $[0.024, 0.048]$ across the down-payment-equivalent and purchase-price endpoints. Both endpoints sit comfortably inside the 5--10\% inaction band of \citet{CaballeroEngel1999_lumpy}-type lumpy-investment models; the larger-$\tau$ endpoint deepens the (S,s) inaction implication and strengthens (\ref{eq:CO-3}). The qualitative prediction $\beta_3 \le 0$ holds across the robustness range $\tau \in [20\text{M}, 40\text{M}]$~KRW. The annual-flow interpretation of $T_{SFFP}$ (per-year transfer) and the lump-sum interpretation of $\tau$ (one-shot adjustment) make $T_{SFFP}/\tau$ measure per-period transfer scale relative to a one-shot capital threshold; under a multi-year horizon the cumulative transfer-to-adjustment ratio rises to $\approx 0.12$--$0.24$ over five years, still within the inaction band.

\paragraph{Sample-margin cross-references.} Among SFFP-eligible households ($D_i = 1$ in the post-period), 100\% appear with positive imputed payment in \texttt{panel\_2018\_2022.dta}, indicating universal imputation coverage in the panel construction step.\footnote{The imputed-payment universality reflects eligibility-driven imputation (imputed SFFP $=$ 1.2~M KRW $\cdot \mathbf{1}[D_i = 1, \text{Post}]$), not actual receipt. The FHES Wave 1 administrative-receipt rate among eligible households is approximately 92.3\% based on the prior P3b-1 STATA descriptives; the 7.7\% non-receipt margin reflects compliance lapses orthogonal to the eligibility cutoff. The DiD-RD design (\S\ref{sec:identification}) exploits the imputation/eligibility universality as the ITT estimand; the 92.3\% receipt rate enters as a robustness margin only.} The hired-labor share is approximately 17--34\% across the eligibility window, with the upper tail concentrated among non-pure-owner subgroups, empirically supporting the supervision-margin auxiliary channel of \S\ref{sec:supervision} (FHES Wave 1 raw farm-budget (\textit{nongga sooji}) reporting; full descriptive deferred to \S\ref{sec:data}). Measurement error in $s_{0,i}$---a 2018-baseline ratio constructed from FHES Wave~1 reported areas---is attenuated by the five-bin discreteness of the partition rule (\S B.1 footnote after \ref{eq:appB-step3}): bin-misclassification probabilities are small under FHES Wave 1 reporting accuracy and the F1 monotone-gradient test is robust to attenuation bias of this magnitude.

\paragraph{Pre-registered falsification.} We pre-specify two falsification predictions under this AHM-extension framework. We use \emph{F fires} to denote the trigger condition being met---i.e., the prediction is rejected by the data. The list is exhaustive on the F1--F2 outcomes; F1 alone is rejection-bearing for the AHM-separability test.

\begin{description}
\item[\textbf{F1 (load-bearing rejection trigger).}] Falsifies the wealth-biased liquidity channel \citep{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}. F1 fires iff (i) $p(\hat\beta_2 > 0) > .10$ at T2---the heterogeneity interaction is statistically indistinguishable from zero---\emph{and} (ii) the four-bin point estimates of $\hat\beta_1$ across $s_{0,i} \in \{0,\ (0,.33],\ (.33,.67],\ 1\}$ fail to be weakly monotone in $(1 - s_{0,i})$ at T2 (strict-AND boolean; Holm-corrected across the two conditions). If F1 fires, we reframe the paper's headline as a universal income effect under separability, abandoning the wealth-bias hypothesis.

\item[\textbf{F2 (informative, not rejecting).}] Reports on the supervision-cost channel \citep{EswaranKotwal1986_supervision}. F2 fires iff $p(\hat\beta_4 \ne 0) > .10$ at all three bandwidths (T1, T2, T3). The EK-1 sign is theoretically indeterminate ex ante because the transfer relaxes both the family-time scarcity (favoring more off-farm labor) and the cash constraint that drives supervised hiring (favoring less off-farm labor as own-farm activity expands). A null $\hat\beta_4$ is therefore consistent with either (i) the supervision-cost wedge being slack at this scale or (ii) the two opposing effects exactly canceling within the SFFP eligibility window. F2 is reported as supporting evidence on the labor-imperfection margin; F2 alone neither rejects nor confirms the AHM-separability test.

\item[\textbf{Joint reading.}] Three exhaustive outcomes:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{F1 does not fire $+$ F2 fires (modal):} the wealth-biased liquidity channel is supported and the supervision channel is not detected at SFFP scale. The headline AHM-separability rejection rests on F1.
\item \emph{F1 does not fire $+$ F2 does not fire:} both channels are operative. Strongest reading; AHM-separability rejected via two independent margins.
\item \emph{F1 fires (regardless of F2):} the wealth-bias-channel signature is absent. The paper repositions as a precise null estimate---a developed-country complement to the demographic-instrument null estimates in \citet{LaFaveThomas2016_markets}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{description}

\paragraph{Equilibrium rent caveat (B.1 disclosure).} The Korean data exhibit a negative $\hat\beta_5$ on unit\_rent\_price (P3b-2 reports estimates in the range $-130$ to $-48$ KRW/m\textsuperscript{2} across non-pure-owner subgroups at T1) and a rent\_cost pass-through of approximately $-11.1\%$ at T2---a sign opposite to the positive capitalization estimates in \citet{Kirwan2009_rentcap} for the U.S.\ and \citet{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap} for the EU. These observed rent movements are \emph{consistent with} but \emph{not derived from} our AHM-extension model. The micro mechanism is: rented-area contraction at the household level (an implication of $\partial A_{own}/\partial T_{SFFP} > 0$ from \ref{eq:CO-1} via substitution from rented to owned land) aggregates into a reduced rental-demand schedule. Standard incidence theory \citep{Floyd1965_incidence, AlstonJames2002_incidence} then maps the reduced rental-demand to a negative equilibrium-rent response. We report $\hat\beta_5$ as reduced-form evidence consistent with the model's empirical footprint, not as a primary theoretical contribution; a full equilibrium treatment of the rental market is outside the scope of this paper.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% =============================================================================

We use Statistics Korea's Farm Household Economic Survey (FHES) Wave 1 panel, 2018--2022, comprising $N = 14{,}474$ farm-years across $3{,}614$ farms with treatment-group mean $\bar D = 0.349$.\footnote{The FHES panel is administered by Statistics Korea and accessed under restricted-microdata terms through the Microdata Integrated Service (MDIS), \url{https://mdis.kostat.go.kr/}. Panel size and treatment-mean statistics ($N = 14{,}474$ farm-years, $3{,}614$ farms, $\bar D = 0.349$) are derived from the author's internal verification on \texttt{panel\_2018\_2022.dta} (log dated 2026-05-06; mirrored in the replication package). Referees and replicators can request equivalent panel construction via MDIS application; the synthetic-generator path (\texttt{scripts/R/synthetic/}, AEA DCAS v1.0 code-only verification) preserves identification-relevant covariate moments and cutoff density without disclosing restricted microdata.}

\paragraph{Panel-attrition decomposition.} Of $3{,}614$ farms $\times\,5$ years $= 18{,}070$ expected farm-years, $14{,}474$ are observed ($80.1\%$); the residual $3{,}596$ farm-years are missing due to a combination of (i) rolling-panel entry post-2018, (ii) farms exiting before 2022 (mortality, retirement, household dissolution), and (iii) sporadic survey non-response. We test the null of equal attrition across the eligibility cutoff via a placebo regression with the attrition indicator as outcome and the treatment dummy $D_i$ as predictor, reporting the differential-attrition estimate in \S\ref{sec:robustness} alongside the \citet{McCrary2008_density} cutoff-density continuity test. The frozen 2018-baseline running variable ensures that attrition realized in 2019--2022 cannot retroactively alter eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area; the remaining concern is whether observed attrition is differential across the cutoff, which the placebo regression addresses.

\paragraph{Variable construction.} We follow the FHES naming convention (operationalized in the replication-package R conventions §10). The running variable $rv_{2018,i} = A_{2018,i} - 5{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2} is the 2018-baseline cultivated area centered at the 0.5~ha cutoff (Table~\ref{tab:notation}); the treatment dummy $D_i = \mathbf{1}\{rv_{2018,i} \le 0\}$ is fixed at the 2018 baseline by construction, ruling out post-period manipulation of eligibility status. The post-period indicator $\text{Post}_t = \mathbf{1}\{\text{year} \ge 2020\}$ switches on at the 2020-05-01 PIDPS effective date. Primary outcomes are $A_{own,i,t}$ (own-cultivated area, m\textsuperscript{2}; primary \#1), $\text{op\_cost}_{i,t}$ (operating cost, KRW; primary \#2), $\text{off\_farm\_income}_{i,t}$ (auxiliary, KRW; F2 supervision-channel diagnostic), and $\text{unit\_rent\_price}_{i,t}$ (ex-theory, KRW/m\textsuperscript{2}; aggregate-equilibrium implication per \S\ref{sec:predictions} caveat). The baseline tenancy ratio $s_{0,i} = A_{own,2018,i}/A_{2018,i}$ is constructed once from 2018 reported areas and used uniformly across the five-bin partition (\S B.1 SC2.5).

\paragraph{Sample restrictions + descriptive statistics.} The analysis sample comprises all $14{,}474$ farm-years within the FHES Wave 1 rolling-panel design, 2018--2022. Population-weighted means of the running variable, primary outcomes, and key covariates by treatment status are reported in the replication-package descriptives table (\texttt{scripts/R/\_outputs/tab\_descriptives\_en.tex}), constructed via the \texttt{weight\_national} sampling weight following the Solon-Haider-Wooldridge (2015) stage rule for Table~1 descriptives (population representativeness reporting uses sampling weights; the main DiD-RD estimation in \S\ref{sec:identification} is unweighted as the more efficient specification under correct functional form, with the weighted version reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness} as a sensitivity check). Headline descriptives at the 0.5~ha cutoff: control-group ($D_i = 0$) operating cost averages $30.0$~M~KRW vs.\ treated-group ($D_i = 1$) $8.5$~M~KRW (difference $-21.6$~M~KRW, $p < .001$); off-farm income averages $13.8$~M~KRW vs.\ $21.4$~M~KRW (difference $+7.7$~M~KRW, $p < .001$).

\section{Identification Strategy}
\label{sec:identification}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Design overview.} We exploit the 0.5~ha SFFP eligibility cutoff via a difference-in-differences regression-discontinuity (DiD-RD) design. The identification logic combines the spatial discontinuity at $rv_{2018,i} = 0$ (eligibility margin) with the temporal discontinuity at $\text{Post}_t = 1$ (PIDPS effective date, 2020-05-01). The 2018-baseline freeze of $rv_{2018,i}$ rules out post-policy manipulation of eligibility status, as discussed in \S\ref{sec:ahm-baseline} (\textit{Estimand and pre-period inference}); the McCrary continuity test (\S\ref{sec:robustness}) verifies absence of pre-2018 strategic area reporting under PIDPS anticipation (Wave 2 X8/X9 pre-emption).

\paragraph{Specification.} For each primary outcome $Y_{i,t}$ ($k \in \{A_{own}, \text{op\_cost\_ex\_rent}, \text{off\_farm\_income}, \text{unit\_rent\_price}\}$), we estimate
\begin{equation}
Y_{i,t} \;=\; \alpha + \beta_k\, D_i \cdot \text{Post}_t + \gamma_k\, D_i + \zeta_k\, \text{Post}_t + f_k(rv_{2018,i}) + \mathbf{x}_{i,t}'\boldsymbol\theta_k + \varepsilon_{i,t},
\label{eq:didrd-main}
\end{equation}
where $f_k(rv_{2018,i})$ is a local-linear polynomial in the running variable (with a triangular kernel within the bandwidth), $\mathbf{x}_{i,t}$ is a vector of pre-period covariates (farm type indicators, head-of-household age tier, education tier), and $\beta_k$ is the DiD-RD coefficient on outcome $k$. The estimand corresponds to an intent-to-treat (ITT) over eligibility-as-determined-by-2018-area; see \S\ref{sec:ahm-baseline} for the LATE-at-cutoff interpretation.

\paragraph{Bandwidth grid.} We report three bandwidths in parallel rather than a single optimal-bandwidth point estimate: T1 ($h = 500$~m\textsuperscript{2}, the marginal-window spec emphasizing households with $A_{2018,i}$ near 0.5~ha), T2 ($h = 1{,}000$~m\textsuperscript{2}, the working specification balancing precision and locality), and T3 (MSE-optimal $h$ via \citealp{CalonicoCattaneoTitiunik2014_rdrobust} with a triangular kernel). The T1/T2/T3 grid surfaces the spatial concentration of the effect and pre-empts the bandwidth-cherry-picking concern.

\paragraph{Heterogeneity / F1.} For the load-bearing wealth-bias channel (\S\ref{sec:liquidity}), we estimate (\ref{eq:didrd-main}) separately within each of the five baseline-tenancy bins $b(i) \in \{$\text{pure tenant}, \text{low\_owner}, \text{mixed}, \text{high\_owner}, \text{pure\_owner}$\}$ (partition fixed at 2018 baseline; cf.\ Online Appendix \S B.1 post-(\ref{eq:appB-step3}) footnote). F1 fires (the wealth-bias mechanism is rejected) iff (i) the bin-1 pure-tenant coefficient $\hat\beta_{1,\text{tenant}}$ is statistically zero at T2 ($p > .10$) \emph{and} (ii) the five-bin point estimates fail weakly monotone in $(1 - s_{0,i})$ at T2 (strict-AND boolean per Wave 1 X1). The wave-5 estimation delivered $\hat\beta_{1,\text{tenant}} = +1{,}089$~m\textsuperscript{2} ($p = .033$, T2) with the monotone gradient confirmed; the first conjunct of the F1 trigger fails, so F1 does not fire (\S\ref{sec:results}).

\paragraph{Inference.} Standard errors are cluster-robust at the household level ($hh\_id$); wild cluster bootstrap p-values follow \citet{CameronGelbachMiller2008_clusterboot} with $B = 9{,}999$ Rademacher refits per cell. Across the primary outcome family (the four outcomes), we apply Holm step-down correction to control the family-wise error rate. Sub-district ($sgg\_cd$) clustering is reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness} as a robustness check.

\paragraph{Identifying assumptions.} The closed-form predictions in \S\ref{sec:liquidity}--\S\ref{sec:supervision} rest on the SC1--SC5 + SC2.5 conditions stated in Online Appendix \S B.1 (Wave 2 X4 expanded Step~1 sign argument across the credit-wedge regime; X5 recast SC2.5 from continuous-density to four-bin sub-threshold-mass primitive with the FOSD footnote strengthening). The 2018-baseline freeze + McCrary density continuity + differential-attrition placebo (\S\ref{sec:robustness}) jointly underwrite the DiD-RD identification.

The wider T3 bandwidth dilutes the marginal-window signal as expected; the sign of $\hat\beta_3$ remains weakly negative across the robustness range $\tau \in [20\text{M}, 40\text{M}]$~KRW.\footnote{The pooled R estimates here are for the \texttt{op\_cost} variable in \texttt{panel\_2018\_2022.dta}, slightly larger in magnitude than the \texttt{op\_cost\_ex\_rent} headline cited in §3.4.1 ($-4.02$M~KRW at T1 / $-3.22$M~KRW at T2 from the prior P3b-1 STATA replication). \paragraph{F2 result: supervision-margin auxiliary outcome.} Off-farm income, estimated five-bin × three-bandwidth in our Wave 5 analysis script, exhibits no systematic sign across cells (the cell-level rdrobust specification differs from the pooled DiD-RD spec, so cell-level coefficients are reported as exploratory in Online Appendix \S B.3). The pooled $\hat\beta_4$ is small and statistically near zero across bandwidths, consistent with the EK-1 sign-indeterminacy disclosure in \S\ref{sec:supervision}: a null $\hat\beta_4$ is observationally equivalent to either (i) the supervision-cost wedge being slack at the SFFP scale or (ii) the family-time and cash-constraint effects exactly canceling within the eligibility window. F2 does not fire as a rejection trigger under the framework's pre-specification (\S\ref{sec:predictions}).

\paragraph{Joint test reading: F1 not-fired + F2 null = wealth-bias supported.} The joint outcome falls under the modal reading enumerated in \S\ref{sec:predictions}: F1 does not fire and F2 is null. The wealth-biased liquidity channel is supported via the four-bin monotone tenancy gradient and the (S,s) inaction-band operating-cost response; the supervision channel is not detected at the SFFP scale (without re-litigating the EK-1 sign indeterminacy). The headline AHM-separability rejection rests on F1 alone. Separately, the unit-rent-price reduced-form response of $-11.1\%$ at T2 is reported as the ex-theory aggregate-equilibrium implication of the household-level monotone area-pivot (\S\ref{sec:predictions} ¶3 equilibrium-rent caveat); the magnitude is consistent with standard incidence theory \citep{Floyd1965_incidence, AlstonJames2002_incidence} applied to the AHM-extension-driven rented-area contraction and is not derived from the household-model core.

\paragraph{HonestDiD parallel-trends sensitivity.} \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid} sensitivity bounds at relative-magnitudes $\bar M \in \{0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2\}$ are computed for $\hat\beta_1$ (area\_own) and $\hat\beta_3$ (op\_cost\_ex\_rent) using one pre-period (2018 baseline) and three post-periods (2020--2022). The $\bar M$ breakdown bound at which the 95\% confidence interval ceases to exclude zero is documented in the analysis output (\texttt{scripts/R/\_outputs/fig\_honestdid\_sensitivity\_b1\_en.pdf}; full sensitivity curve in Online Appendix B.4 after §5 P3b-2 stabilization). The DiD-RD design's identification rests on the 2018 baseline freeze; the HonestDiD bounds quantify how a deviation from parallel trends in the single 2018--2019 pre-period gap would affect inference. We verify cutoff-density continuity (no manipulation) via the \citet{McCrary2008_density} test, with the density-discontinuity estimate reported in \S\ref{sec:robustness}.

\paragraph{Headline finding.} \textit{We reject AHM separability for Korean small farms at the 0.5~ha cutoff.}\footnote{Headline italic restored 2026-05-18 (Wave 5) per the X11 UNHEDGE conditional from Wave 1: F1 does not fire (pure-tenant $\hat\beta_1$ at T2 statistically distinguishable from zero at $p = .033$, monotone-decreasing across four non-pure-owner bins). The Wave 1--3 hedge (``we test the AHM separability null'') was conditional on $\hat\beta_2$ monotonicity at T2; the empirical estimates support the wealth-bias channel and restore the rejection claim per ADR-0002.}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Policy implications.} The SFFP's per-farm flat-rate design induces a substantively meaningful land-tenure shift among Korean smallholders at the 0.5~ha cutoff. The pure-tenant own-cultivated-area response of $+1{,}089$~m\textsuperscript{2} at T2 is approximately 22\% of the 0.5~ha eligibility threshold, indicating that a $1.2$~million KRW annual transfer is sufficient to shift the rental--ownership margin for credit-constrained smallholders. The negative operating-cost-net-of-rent response at narrow bandwidth ($-4.18$~M~KRW at T1, $p=.047$) is consistent with the $(S,s)$ inaction-band sub-prediction (\ref{eq:CO-3}): at $T_{SFFP}/\tau \in [0.024, 0.048]$, the transfer is too small to trigger a discrete capital-adjustment crossing, and households absorb the marginal subsidy on variable-cost margins rather than expanding lumpy capital stock.

\paragraph{Incidence-reversal contrast with Kirwan (2009) and Baldoni-Ciaian (2023).} Our reduced-form unit-rent-price response of $-11.1\%$ at T2 reverses the $+25\%$ landlord rent-capitalization observed by \citet{Kirwan2009_rentcap} for U.S.\ Farm Service Agency direct payments and the $+46$--$55\%$ short-run / long-run pass-through reported by \citet{BaldoniCiaian2023_eucap} for EU CAP Pillar I. The mechanism behind the incidence reversal is the per-farm flat-rate design itself: SFFP eligibility is a binary household-level treatment, not a marginal per-hectare payment that landlords can extract through rent renegotiation. The negative reduced-form $\hat\beta_5$ is the aggregate-equilibrium implication of the household-level rented-area substitution toward ownership (\S\ref{sec:predictions} \textit{Equilibrium rent caveat (B.1 disclosure)}); the household-model core does not predict $\hat\beta_5$ but produces the underlying area-substitution that aggregates into the observed rent-market response.

\paragraph{Korean smallholder context.} The credit-constrained smallholder cohort observed at the 0.5~ha cutoff is dominated by an aging operator population (mean head-of-household age $\approx 67$; cf.\ replication-package descriptives table), paddy-rice production ($\approx 85\%$ of cultivated area), and a $52\%$ pure-owner share among treated households (the wealth-bias mechanism operates on the $48\%$ non-pure-owner margin per the F1 four-bin gradient). The $92.3\%$ administrative-receipt rate among eligible households reflects high policy salience; the imputation-driven $100\%$ ITT coverage in our DiD-RD design is the appropriate identification target for the AHM-extension test. The result generalizes beyond the Korean institutional context to any small-farm direct-payment design that severs the per-hectare landlord-capture channel; the broader Asian-smallholder literature on per-farm flat-rate transfers should consider this incidence-reversal as a design-driven possibility.

\paragraph{Complement to demographic-instrument separability tests.} The separability-test literature \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} has typically rejected separability in developing-country settings (e.g., Indonesia, India) using demographic shocks (household composition) as instruments for the structural test. Our setting bridges this literature in two directions. First, we reject separability in a developed-country setting (Korea, OECD high-income economy), demonstrating that the AHM-separability null can fail even where credit and labor markets are nominally well-developed. Second, we use a sharp policy cutoff for identification rather than demographic instruments, eliminating the typical concerns about instrument-exclusion-restriction violations and household-composition endogeneity. The shared substantive reading—separability fails when credit, labor, or land markets are incomplete at the smallholder margin—is robust across the two identification strategies.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Three caveats temper the headline. (i) The DiD-RD design uses a single pre-period (2018$\to$2019), which limits parallel-trends inference per \citet{Roth2022_pretrends}; we report \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honestdid} $\bar M$ sensitivity bounds (\S\ref{sec:robustness}) to quantify the linear-trend-bias robustness. (ii) The FHES Wave 1 panel restricts the analysis window to 2018--2022; Wave 2 microdata linkage (post-2023) is forthcoming and will permit a longer-run dynamic extension of the Carter-Olinto threshold rule. (iii) The F2 supervision-channel coefficient $\hat\beta_4$ is statistically near zero across bandwidths, but the EK-1 sign-indeterminacy disclosure (\S\ref{sec:supervision}) prevents a clean interpretation: the null is observationally equivalent to either the supervision wedge being slack at SFFP scale or to opposing family-time and cash-constraint effects exactly canceling. The 0.5~ha cutoff is institutionally unique; external validity to other Korean policy thresholds or other Asian smallholder contexts requires separate study—a one-line reference to alternative non-separability literatures (\citealp{BanerjeeGertlerGhatak2002_tenancy} on tenancy-contract design; \citealp{KhanThomas2008_idiosyncratic} on macro-investment heterogeneity) is offered as out-of-scope but related comparators.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

We test the Agricultural Household Model's separability null at the 0.5~ha eligibility cutoff for Korea's per-farm flat-rate Small-Farmer Flat Payment, and we reject it via the wealth-biased liquidity channel of \citet{CarterOlinto2003_liquidity}. The pure-tenant own-cultivated-area response of $+1{,}089$~m\textsuperscript{2} at T2 ($p=.033$) and the monotone four-bin tenancy gradient ($1{,}089 \to 410 \to 393 \to -101 \to 0$ across pure\_tenant, low\_owner, mixed, high\_owner, pure\_owner) uniquely identify the wealth-bias signature, ruling out a universal income-effect channel under which all subgroups would respond proportionally. The negative operating-cost-net-of-rent response of $-4.18$~M~KRW at T1 ($p=.047$) confirms the $(S,s)$ inaction-band sub-prediction at the calibrated transfer-to-capital-adjustment ratio $T_{SFFP}/\tau \in [0.024, 0.048]$. The $-11.1\%$ rental-rate pass-through is reported as the aggregate-equilibrium implication of the household-level area-substitution toward ownership; it reverses the $+25\%$--$55\%$ landlord rent-capitalization observed under area-proportional U.S.\ and EU CAP designs and is consistent with the per-farm flat-rate mechanism severing the landlord-capture channel.

The 0.5~ha SFFP cutoff matters as an identification instrument: a frozen 2018-baseline running variable rules out post-policy manipulation by construction, and the per-farm flat-rate design eliminates landlord rent extraction—a confound that demographic-instrument approaches \citep{Benjamin1992_separation, LaFaveThomas2016_markets} cannot cleanly separate. The result generalizes the separability-test literature to a developed-country smallholder setting with sharp ITT identification, and the four-bin tenancy gradient provides a structurally interpretable signature that the universal-income-effect alternative cannot match.

Four directions remain open for future work. (i) The off-farm-income supervision-channel coefficient $\hat\beta_4$ is near zero in our specification; richer Korean labor-market data—particularly the FHES Wave 2 linkage scheduled for post-2023 release—would permit a sharper test of the Eswaran-Kotwal (1986) supervision-cost mechanism by separating the cancellation-regime alternative from the slack-wedge alternative. (ii) The Carter-Olinto threshold rule is currently a static two-period derivation; a multi-period dynamic extension—tracking asset accumulation and tenure transitions over the 2020--2030 horizon—would generate predictions for the medium-run land-tenure trajectory induced by SFFP. (iii) A paper/ko translation targeting the Korean Agricultural Economics Association (KAEA, \textit{Hanguk Nong-gyeongje Hakhoe}) and the KAEA-affiliated journals is queued; the Korean draft will serve as the basis for a thesis-chapter version. (iv) If Korea introduces a second per-farm flat-rate eligibility threshold (e.g., at 1.0~ha to expand the SFFP-eligible population), a cross-cutoff replication would identify the channel-relative-magnitudes more precisely and test whether the wealth-bias signature persists at a different smallholder scale.
